## Unavailable Skills

The following skills are **not available** to Regency characters:

- Computer Use
- Drive Auto
- Electrical Repair
- Electronics
- Hypnosis *(replaced by Mesmerism)*
- Pilot (most specializations)
- Psychoanalysis
- Operate Heavy Machinery
- Science (all specializations) *(replaced by Natural Philosophy)*

## New & Adjusted Skills

### Astronomy (01%)

Replaces Science specializations related to astronomy. Covers star gazing, navigation by the stars, astrology, and some meteorology. Listed as a separate skill in its own right.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** identify common star systems; navigate by the stars; common astrological beliefs.
- **Hard difficulty:** identify uncommon celestial phenomena; lesser-known astrological beliefs.

**Pushing examples:** taking more time to study; conducting further research; consulting another expert.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** misdirected or lost due to inability to read the stars; give a wrong interpretation of astrology. If insane, the investigator's rationality or beliefs may crumble.

---

### Credit Rating (00%)

Determines an investigator's relative wealth. For the landed gentry and aristocracy, wealth is primarily determined by land ownership. In Regency Cthulhu, Credit Rating also infers an investigator's place in society. An investigator's social class — lower, emerging middle, or upper (gentry and aristocracy) — is determined by their Living Standards, based on their Credit Rating skill, governed by their occupation.

Each player should check their investigator's chosen occupation to see which occupational band it suggests. They must invest enough skill points to at least meet the minimum Credit Rating value for their band (e.g., 50 if a Gentleman or Gentlewoman). They may spend occupational or personal interest skill points to improve Credit Rating, but only up to the maximum for that occupational band.

---

### Dancing (DEX/5)

Base skill percentage = DEX ÷ 5. Covers looking good on the dancefloor and moving gracefully with a partner. Both ladies and gentlemen are judged on their dancing, and it is a powerful way to impress a potential paramour.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** impress a partner or general company; acquit oneself well on the dancefloor.
- **Hard difficulty:** impress in particularly stressful situations (e.g., in the company of royalty); demonstrate an innovative dance move.

**Pushing examples:** begging a partner's pardon and restarting the dance; asking for a second dance to make good.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** using the wrong moves suggests drunkenness; falling over and looking like a "complete arse"; losing 1D6+ Reputation points. If insane, the investigator may become consumed with the dance, creating "new" flirtatious moves, or be lost in the music and considered mad.

---

### Drive Carriage/Cart (20%)

Replaces Drive Auto. Applies to any form of carriage, from small buggies to large four-horse coaches. For lower-class characters such as farmers, the skill is Drive Cart rather than Drive Carriage.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** successfully drive the horses while avoiding obstacles.
- **Hard difficulty:** successfully drive the horses while avoiding obstacles in poor conditions.

**Pushing examples:** driving the horses and carriage/cart to their limit; ignoring risk or the comfort of the passengers.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** crashing; injuring the horses; injuring passengers; losing luggage. If insane, the investigator may think themselves one of the horses and attempt to pull the carriage by hand.

---

### Etiquette (INT/5)

Base skill percentage = INT ÷ 5. Essential for anyone in high society. Governs who you can talk to, when, and how you address them. **Replaces the use of Credit Rating for social influencing rolls**, as affluence is not an accurate representation of manners and social status during the Regency.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** correct forms of address and introduction; knowing one's silverware at dinner.
- **Hard difficulty:** impress those who possess particularly rigid demeanors; observe correct procedures when under pressure or stress.

**Pushing examples:** begging pardon and restating one's credentials.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** losing 1D6 Reputation points; being ostracized; being turned away by the butler; being "black balled" and removed from the guest list. If insane, the investigator may think they are performing the necessary functions when in fact they are behaving wildly and causing a scene.

---

### Fashion (10%)

Covers knowing the correct styles of the season and how to dress well. Before any social event, the Keeper might call for a Fashion roll from each attendee, with the best level of success indicating the best dressed. Applies as much to men as to women — if anything, more so.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** knowing correct attire for the function or meeting; ensuring one is well presented.
- **Hard difficulty:** impressing company with one's attire; dressing quickly when under stress or pressure.

**Pushing examples:** removing oneself to correct a wardrobe malfunction and trying again.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** losing 1D6 Reputation points; being ostracized for wearing the wrong attire; being turned away by the butler. If insane, the investigator may attempt some new "experimental" fashion, or perhaps forget to wear any clothes at all.

---

### Gaming (10%)

Shows the investigator's talent at card games. Cards are a popular pursuit for men and women, although only men are allowed to gamble at late night parties. Popular games include Whist, Loo, Speculation, and Commerce.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Opposed with Gaming or Psychology skill.**
- **Regular difficulty:** able to play card games confidently; understand the rules of the game.
- **Hard difficulty:** learn a new or unfamiliar game; gamble under pressure; cheat.

**Pushing examples:** increasing one's betting risk; proposing a more complex game.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** lose big; losing 1D6+ Reputation points; gain an enemy. If insane, they may be caught cheating or using different rules (much to everyone's shock and disdain).

---

### Mesmerism (01%) [Uncommon]

Replaces Hypnosis. Induces a trancelike state causing heightened suggestibility, relaxation, and possible recall/repression of memories. Opposed roll: user's Mesmerism vs. target's POW or Psychology (willing targets need only the mesmerist to succeed). Only affects one target at a time. Devious/malign use costs Sanity points (Keeper's discretion).

**Three uses:**

1. **Alleviate pain:** Character performs as though uninjured for 1D6 rounds (even for major wounds). After this period, wounds are fully felt; Keeper may call for a CON roll to avoid unconsciousness.
2. **Suppress mental trauma:** Recipient recovers +1D3 Sanity points (cannot exceed max SAN) by instilling a mental "block." Failed roll = no recovery. Fumbled/failed pushed roll = –1D6 Sanity loss as trauma is relived. A series of successful sessions (min. 1D6, Keeper's discretion) may cure indefinite insanity or a phobia/mania.
3. **Plant misinformation:** Alter the memory of an NPC. The Keeper may supply false replies to future inquiries regarding those memories. An unscrupulous mesmerist may thoroughly confuse real and manufactured recollections.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Opposed with POW or Psychology skill.**

**Pushing examples:** ensuring no distractions; using a prop (e.g., a gem or pocket watch).

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** a past memory/trauma surfaces, causing –1D6 Sanity loss; target becomes a risk to themselves. If insane, the mesmerist becomes "self-mesmerized" and falls catatonic for 1D6 hours (penalty die to skill rolls until recovered).

---

### Natural Philosophy (01%)

Replaces the Science skill. **Has no specializations.** Covers an undisciplined understanding of physics, biology, and chemistry, with a touch of alchemy and advanced mathematics. Knowledge comes from collected works of other academics (e.g., Newton) rather than defined disciplines.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** conduct an experiment; aware of current thinking.
- **Hard difficulty:** conducting a demanding or risky experiment.

**Pushing examples:** taking more time; conducting further research; consulting an expert.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** experiment goes awry; damage is caused. If insane, the investigator may explore a strange field of study or question accepted beliefs.

---

### Reassure (APP/5)

Base skill percentage = APP ÷ 5. The Regency-era substitute for psychoanalysis — calm words and intuitive insight from a caring person.

- Successful roll: recipient recovers **+3 Sanity points** (cannot exceed max SAN).
- Failed: no Sanity recovered.
- Fumbled/failed pushed: **–1D6 Sanity loss**.
- Successful use **halves** the length of temporary insanity recovery.
- Can allow a character to ignore a phobia or mania for approximately **1D6 minutes**.
- Can help a person with a delusion (from underlying insanity) see through their hallucination.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** help someone overcome mental trauma.
- **Hard difficulty:** help someone when the cause is still present, or the situation is desperate/dangerous.

**Pushing examples:** taking more time; removing to a safer or calmer place.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** target suffers –1D6 Sanity loss; target reacts badly and attacks.

---

### Religion (10%)

Covers knowledge of human religions, belief systems, mythologies — doctrine, practice, and history. Core skill focuses on "main world religions" (Christianity, Judaism, Hinduism, Islam, Buddhism, Shinto, Sikhism, etc.). Additional specialties may be taken (e.g., Religion (Paganism), (Berber), (Cherokee)).

Uses: identify/enter a sacred space, join a ritual respectfully, address a practitioner correctly, understand key mythologies, identify a text as heterodox or heretical.

- Using in relation to a fringe/obscure belief system requires a **Hard success** unless the investigator has taken the specific specialty.

**Opposing skill/Difficulty level:**
- **Regular difficulty:** understand a religion's core beliefs, history, and forms of address.
- **Hard difficulty:** identify religious accoutrements associated with a lesser-known spiritual practice.

**Pushing examples:** taking more time; further research; consulting an expert; participating in a spiritual retreat.

**Failed pushed roll consequences:** offence caused; hostile attention drawn; mistaken information leading to a blunder. If insane, the investigator's own faith may crumble or they undergo radical conversion.

## Skill Specializations

Certain modern/classic specializations are not available. Only the following should be used:

### Art/Craft

**Art (for the gentry and aristocracy):** Acting, Art Appreciation, Embroidery, Fine Art, Forgery, Music (Piano, Violin, Singing), Poetry, Sculpture.

**Craft (for the working classes):** Blacksmithing, Carpentry, Cooking, Pottery, Sewing.

### Fighting

Axe, Brawl, Flail, Garrote, Spear, Sword (Rapier or Saber), Whip.

### Firearms

Bow, Pistol (replaces Handgun), Rifle/Blunderbuss (replaces Rifle/Shotgun).

## Uncommon Skills

- **Animal Handling (05%):** Very common, but only for the lower classes.
- **Artillery (01%):** Covers cannon use on the battlefield. Common for military characters; add to occupational skills for all military careers.
- **Demolitions (01%):** Available, but explosives are much less powerful than the 1920s. Common for Miners.
- **Diving:** Considered part of Swimming; no separate skill.
- **Lore (01%):** Available, but many subject options are covered by Occult and Natural Philosophy.
- **Pilot (Boat) (01%):** Only common for lower classes (fisher-/ferrymen) and navy.
- **Read Lips (01%):** Exceptionally rare; available with Keeper's permission, particularly for Spies.

## New Occupations (Full Stats)

### Gentleman

A member of the landed gentry who lives their life as they please due to the benefits of wealth and privilege.

- **Occupation Skill Points:** (EDU × 2) + (APP × 2)
- **Occupational Band:** 4 (Gentry/Nouveau Riche)
- **Credit Rating:** 50–90
- **Skills:** Charm, Dancing, Etiquette, Fashion, Gaming, Ride, one weapon skill, plus one other skill as a personal hobby.

---

### Gentlewoman

The daughter of a gentleman and a member of the landed gentry. Barred from owning any possessions and reliant on their family for financial support. Most gentlewomen are simply waiting to marry.

- **Occupation Skill Points:** (EDU × 2) + (APP × 2)
- **Occupational Band:** 4 (Gentry/Nouveau Riche)
- **Credit Rating:** 50–90
- **Skills:** Art/Craft (Embroidery, Fine Art, Instrument, Singing, etc.), Dancing, Etiquette, Fashion, Other Language (any — usually French), Persuade, Ride, plus one other skill as a personal hobby.

---

### Nouveau Riche

Many families have made a fortune as merchants — more than enough for their wealth to match that of the landed gentry, and in some cases, even the aristocracy. While they are regarded as interlopers in high society, those with the right manners and connections can often find acceptance.

- **Occupation Skill Points:** EDU × 4
- **Occupational Band:** 4 (Gentry/Nouveau Riche)
- **Credit Rating:** 50–90
- **Skills:** Accounting, Appraise, Art/Craft (Business), Dancing, Fast Talk, Persuade, Ride, one weapon skill.

---

### Servant (Footman)

Male servants mostly used for lifting, carrying, and looking pretty. Dressed in the uniform of the house (livery), they carry luggage, convey messages, help gentlemen dress, and assist in serving at dinner.

- **Occupation Skill Points:** (INT × 2) + (POW × 2)
- **Occupational Band:** 1 (Laborer/Servant)
- **Credit Rating:** 10–30
- **Skills:** Animal Handling, Art/Craft (Any), Dodge, Etiquette, Fast Talk, Listen, Ride, Spot Hidden.

---

### Servant (Housemaid)

Housemaids see to the house under the direction of the Housekeeper. They rise before the family, clean floors, dust shelves, build fires, do laundry, wait on the family, help ladies dress, and serve at dinner.

- **Occupation Skill Points:** (INT × 2) + (POW × 2)
- **Occupational Band:** 1 (Laborer/Servant)
- **Credit Rating:** 10–30
- **Skills:** Art/Craft (Cooking or Sewing), Dodge, Etiquette, Fashion, Fast Talk, Listen, Spot Hidden, Stealth.

---

## Occupational Bands

### Band 1: Laborer/Servant (CR 9%–30%)

Almost at the bottom of the social heap. Working-class laborers and servants. A Butler's or Housekeeper's Credit Rating is capped at 40% to reflect their higher status within a household.

**Occupations:** Burglar, Butler/Valet/Maid, Criminal, Driver (Carriage), Miner, Missionary, Sailor, Servant (Footman), Servant (Housemaid), Smuggler, Soldier/Marine, Zealot.

### Band 2: Shopkeeper/Craftsperson (CR 20%–60%)

Better-off working-class tradespeople or business owners. As arts are classed as a craft, this band includes artistic occupations — game play and background may permit their CR cap to be raised to Professional (70%), with Keeper's permission.

**Occupations:** Actor, Antique Dealer, Archaeologist, Artist, Author, Book Dealer, Boxer, Craftsperson, Entertainer, Farmer, Gambler, Journalist, Librarian, Museum Curator, Musician, Occultist, Pharmacist, Prostitute, Shopkeeper, Spy, Undertaker, Zookeeper.

### Band 3: Professional (CR 30%–70%)

Doctors, lawyers, members of the clergy, poets, scientists/natural philosophers. Academic professions are respected and often move in wealthier circles. With thorough education deemed "unsuitable" for women, this class may be almost exclusively male (depending on style of game).

**Occupations:** Accountant, Antiquarian, Architect, Clergy (Member of the), Doctor, Explorer, Lawyer, Military Officer, Professor, Researcher.

### Band 4: Gentry/Nouveau Riche (CR 50%–90%)

The well-born earn money from the land they own and do not need to work at all. The patriarch controls all the money. The most successful tradesmen and industrialists may also achieve this level of wealth but rarely acquire the social cachet.

**Occupations:** Gentleman, Gentlewoman, Nouveau Riche.

### Band 5: Aristocracy (CR 90%–100%)

Not routinely available to investigators — included for completeness. The titled aristocracy are the one percent. To create aristocratic investigators, rename Gentleman/Gentlewoman to Nobleman/Noblewoman and adjust Credit Rating to 90%–100%.

## Living Standards

| Credit Rating | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | **Destitute.** Nothing beyond rags. Beg for scraps, sleep in doorways, turn to crime. |
| 1–9% | **Poor.** Little or no money, meanest of rented accommodation, own only clothes on your back. |
| 10–29% | **Lower-Class.** A place to live and a job, but only enough to feed your family and keep a basic roof. |
| 30–49% | **Emerging Middle-Class / Starting Professional.** Established business, trade, or profession. Decent living, a few low-class luxuries. |
| 50–69% | **Poor Gentry / Established Professional.** Lowest end of the wealthiest circles. A family estate, but small and not well-appointed. (cf. Elinor & Marianne Dashwood, the Bennet family) |
| 70–82% | **Average Gentry / Wealthy Professional.** Standard gentry wealth. Good carriage, new muslin ball-gowns each season. Full estate with a manor house large enough for entertaining. |
| 83–89% | **Wealthy Gentry.** High end of social class, almost aristocracy. Vast estate, stately family home. (cf. Darcy, Emma Woodhouse) |
| 90–100% | **Aristocracy.** Land, title, connections to the royal family. Estate better described as a palace. |

## Estate Standards

Roll 1D6 to determine the size and grandeur of a family estate. Ignore any result that doesn't make sense for the investigator's Living Standard.

| Die Roll (1D6) | Standard | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Poor | A small estate, or at least one overcrowded with children. It has a small manor house and a few tenants working the land. |
| 2 | Comfortable | Not much larger than a poor one, but provides a better income and looks quaint rather than small. |
| 3 | Charming | Provides very well for the family with good social standing. Not large enough for sizable gatherings. |
| 4 | Large | A true stately home, lauded for historical value rather than size or comfort. Owned for generations. Can hold large social events but not too many. |
| 5 | Impressive | May not be much bigger than a large estate, but impeccably maintained enough to attract visitors. Would be a crime not to host at least one sizable social gathering per year. |
| 6 | Grand | Very large house with expansive grounds and land. The family are one of the wealthiest in the area. |

## Reputation

*(Optional rule — applies to gentry, pseudo-gentry, and those pretending to be such)*

**Starting Reputation** = (Credit Rating + Etiquette) ÷ 2. Maximum = 99.

| Reputation Score | Society's Opinion | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Irredeemably wicked | Difficulty level of the roll is Extreme |
| 1–39 | A bad sort | Dice roll incurs a penalty die |
| 40–59 | Perfectly average and respectable | No penalty or bonus |
| 60–98 | A credit to the "Ton" (high society) | Dice roll gains a bonus die |
| 99 | Positively angelic | Two bonus dice or automatic pass (Keeper's discretion) |

## Regency Costume Glossary

| Item | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Bonnet** | Peaked women's headgear, worn on the back of the head and frequently tied under the chin with a ribbon. Could be made of straw or fabric, plain or highly decorated. |
| **Breeches** | Male attire covering waist to knee. Fastened with buttons, buckles, or drawstrings. Various fabrics depending on social class. |
| **Chemise** | A plain, short-sleeved white cotton undergarment worn by women. Also known as a "shift." Shorter than any overdresses. |
| **Chemisette** | A sleeveless "little chemise" (under blouse) worn during the day to fill the neckline of an empire line gown. Often linen, muslin, or lace. |
| **Cravat** | A necktie or neckband for men, developed from military clothing. Invariably white linen, silk, or cambric cloth. Could be tied in many ways. |
| **Empire line** | High-waisted women's gown with a long flowing skirt and fitted bodice, inspired by Neoclassical fashion. Neckline often cut daringly low. Muslin and silk were fashionable. |
| **Fichu** | A triangular or folded square shawl, usually lace or linen, worn across the shoulders and knotted or crossed in front. Preserves modesty. |
| **Frock coat** | Tailored men's coat originally for sport. The "dress coat" (single/double-breasted, cut away at front, high waist, with tails) was most common. Also the "morning coat" with sloping, rounded front edge. Buttons often just for show. |
| **Mittens (mitts)** | Fingerless gloves with half-thumb, usually worn by women. Various lengths and fabrics (cotton, lace). Could be worn at all times indoors and out. |
| **Mob cap** | Soft fabric headgear (often lace) worn by upper-class married women, mothers, widows, servants, and old maids. Mostly indoors; could be worn under a bonnet. Often trimmed with ruffles, ribbons, embroidery. |
| **Pantaloons** | Ankle-length trousers originally worn by working classes but introduced into fashionable society in the 1790s by Beau Brummell. Often held in place with stirrups. |
| **Pelisse** | A long-sleeved, high-waisted coat for outdoor wear. Full length during the Regency period. |
| **Petticoat** | Worn over the chemise and stays. Sleeveless and longer than the overlying dress; hem often decorated with ruffles or lace. |
| **Reticule** | A small handbag or purse with drawstring closure. Essential as the empire line's delicate fabrics were unsuitable for pockets. Made of net, silk, velvet, or satin. |
| **Spencer** | A short, high-waisted jacket to complement the empire line. Originally men's, adopted by gentlewomen. Often coupled with a full-length hooded (frequently red) woolen cloak. |
| **Stays** | A type of corset worn over the chemise. The empire line did not require corsetry as standard. "Short stays" (ending below bust) were most common. "Long stays" to slim the figure were different from Victorian corsets. |
| **Stockings** | Also known as hose. Worn by men and women. Made of silk or wool, held up with garters. |
| **Turban, draped** | Women's fabric headgear, fashionable during the Regency. Inspired by Turkey and the Indian subcontinent. Could be decorated with feathers and jewels for evening wear. |
| **Waistcoat** | Single or double-breasted male garment worn over the shirt. High-waisted, square-cut bottom. Lapel and collar height varied over the Regency period. Silk, satin, and wool were popular. |
