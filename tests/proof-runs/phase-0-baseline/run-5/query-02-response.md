## Reginald Osterbrook Fitch

**Concept:** A once-respected antiquarian dealer whose mind cracked a decade ago when he opened the wrong sealed crate — now he knows the wrong things, drinks too much, and jumps at shadows that are sometimes real.

**Appearance:** Mid-fifties, formerly well-dressed but gone to seed: a good-quality Edwardian waistcoat worn shiny at the elbows, ink-stained fingers that tremble slightly when at rest. He keeps his back to walls and always sits facing the door. His eyes are permanently red-rimmed, as if he hasn't had a full night's sleep in years — because he hasn't.

**Portrayal:** Fitch begins every conversation at a controlled, formal register — the professional antiquarian persona is a survival mechanism. Then something the investigators say trips a wire and his sentences shorten, his voice drops, and he starts glancing at the nearest exit. When badly frightened he resorts to Latin under his breath (not prayer — classifications, taxonomy, as if labelling things keeps them contained).

**Voice:** Speaks in precise, appraising language ("this piece dates to the third dynasty of the subject kingdom — that is, if it *has* a kingdom"). Qualifies everything obsessively, as if the act of precision can ward off uncertainty. Mid-conversation, precision breaks down into fragments.

### AIMS Profile

**Agenda:**
- *Immediate:* Get these investigators out of his shop before they attract whatever they've brought with them. Determine whether they are dangerous fools, merely dangerous, or potentially useful.
- *Short-term:* Acquire and destroy a specific catalogue of Mythos-adjacent artefacts before someone else does — he doesn't trust institutions, repositories, or other dealers.
- *Long-term:* Find the journal of a colleague who disappeared in 1919, which Fitch believes contains the key to understanding what he saw in that crate — and whether it can be sealed back up again.

**Instinct:** Freeze first, then flee. Under genuine supernatural pressure he locks up completely for 1-2 rounds before bolting. He has survived this long not by courage but by *leaving* — he has an almost preternatural ability to sense when a situation is about to go badly and exit before it does. Against human threat: fawn and misdirect.

**Moves (if PCs don't intervene):**
1. Sends an anonymous letter to the British Museum's Near East department alerting them a colleague is holding "material of contested provenance" — his way of triggering a legal headache that forces the colleague to move the item before it draws worse attention.
2. Closes his Kensington shop "temporarily for re-cataloguing" and relocates to his Dorset cottage, where he believes distance from London's underground trade reduces exposure.
3. Contacts an old Cambridge acquaintance (Professor Agatha Hellmuth) who has since become unreachable — he doesn't know she's already been claimed.

**Secrets:**
- *Surface:* visibly nervous, jumpy about window draughts, keeps his stock-room padlocked with two locks. Regularly refuses to sell certain items regardless of price.
- *Investigation (Psychology or 1-2 sessions of relationship):* He has classified roughly forty artefacts in his possession as "non-commercial" — items he won't sell, won't display, and won't discuss, but cannot bring himself to destroy. He believes destroying them is what they want him to do.
- *Deep (full investigation, his private journals, or a Hard Psychology roll at a moment of trust):* In 1914 he assisted in the cataloguing of a private Levantine collection brought to London. One crate was sealed with compound locks and a wax seal inscribed in a language he couldn't identify. He opened it. The contents were *not* artefacts in any normal sense. He re-sealed the crate, falsified the catalogue entry, and buried it in a Suffolk field. He knows where it is. He believes something has been trying to find it ever since, which is why it hasn't found him: he's too useful as a lead, not as a target. His paranoia is, in significant part, correct.

### Relationships

- **Professor Agatha Hellmuth** (Cambridge, Classics): Trusted colleague — would share almost anything with her. Now unreachable. Fitch is worried but rationalising. (Already dead or worse; hook for investigators.)
- **Gerald Stoate** (auction house runner, Sotheby's): Semi-trusted fence for legitimate pieces. Fitch uses him to quietly offload items he wants out of his possession. Stoate doesn't know why certain items always come cheap.
- **Unnamed Levantine contact** ("M."): The dealer who sold him the 1914 collection. Believed dead. Fitch is wrong — M. is alive, changed, and has been looking for Fitch for nine years.
- **Investigators:** Initially classified as "probably dangerous amateurs who will get themselves killed and possibly attract attention to me in the process." Upgrades to useful if they demonstrate genuine competence. May become the first people he's trusted in a decade if they handle something he can't handle alone.

### GM Notes

**Running Fitch:** He rewards patience. Investigators who push hard will get deflection and then nothing — he's had years to develop excuses. Investigators who give him a reason to trust them (demonstrate they already know something is real, or handle a threat he couldn't) will get access to one layer of secrets per session of careful relationship-building.

**The useful lie:** Fitch will tell investigators that he has *no idea* what became of the 1914 collection — and he almost believes it. When pushed, he will admit the crate exists. He will not, under ordinary circumstances, tell anyone where it is. A SAN loss event he witnesses alongside investigators may break this resistance.

**Sanity trigger:** If investigators produce an image, rubbing, or description of the wax seal inscription from the 1914 crate (which they might find in other sources), Fitch makes an immediate SAN roll (0/1D4). On a failure, he becomes briefly incoherent and then extraordinarily forthcoming — the denial mechanism breaks. This is the designed pivot point.

**Threat level to investigators:** None directly. His knowledge is the danger. Getting close to Fitch is what draws M.'s attention back to him — and whatever M. has become will not observe the social niceties.

**If Fitch dies:** His private catalogue in the briefcase is the critical inheritance. It contains enough cross-referenced material to function as a partial substitute for what he knew, plus the location of the buried crate. Investigators who search his shop will find the forty "non-commercial" items in a locked back room: a small cabinet of horrors that will each require decisions about what to do with them.
