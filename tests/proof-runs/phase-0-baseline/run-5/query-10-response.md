## Edvard Moll
Concept: A mid-level clerk or records keeper who knows more than he should and is terrified of what that knowledge costs him.

Appearance: Heavyset, perpetually damp with perspiration — not from exertion, just from the effort of maintaining composure. Wears a coat a size too large, as though he bought it to hide something inside.

Portrayal: Every answer comes a beat too late. He smiles at the wrong moments — not warmly, but reflexively, like it's a nervous tic. If the players push, he physically turns slightly sideways, as if he's angling toward a door that isn't there.

Voice: Speaks in qualifications. "I couldn't say for certain." "That might be the case, I suppose." "Someone else would know better than I." He never commits to a statement he can't walk back.

---

AIMS:
- **Agenda:**
  - *Immediate:* Get through this conversation without saying anything actionable. Appear cooperative while giving nothing away.
  - *Short-term:* Warn his contact that investigators are asking questions, then go dark for a while.
  - *Long-term:* Find a way out of whatever arrangement he's trapped in before it kills him.
- **Instinct:** Fawn first, then flee. He appeases authority reflexively before calculating whether it's safe to do so.
- **Moves:**
  - Volunteers a minor, plausible-sounding detail that sends investigators down a safe dead end.
  - Immediately after the scene ends, sends an unsigned note to his contact describing what the PCs asked.
  - If cornered, offers to "find some documents" — genuinely useful ones — in exchange for being left alone.
- **Secrets:**
  - *Surface:* He's nervous and has something on his mind. A Spot Hidden / Notice / Perception check catches him glancing at a specific drawer or document shelf.
  - *Investigation:* He received a payment six weeks ago from a name that shouldn't exist — a shell account or a recently-dead person's estate.
  - *Deep:* He witnessed something (a meeting, a delivery, a disposal) and agreed to stay quiet. He has a single piece of physical evidence — a ledger page, a wax seal, a receipt — hidden at home. He has been waiting for someone brave enough to give him a reason to hand it over.

---

Relationships:
- **Unknown contact (antagonist faction):** Pays Edvard to maintain silence and provide early warning of investigators. He does not know who this person ultimately reports to.
- **A colleague or supervisor:** Oblivious — Edvard has gone to lengths to appear normal around them. Could vouch for his character, which makes the players' suspicion feel unfair at first.
- **The PCs (potential):** If they treat him with patience rather than pressure, he can become an informant. He's not loyal to anyone — he just wants to survive.

---

**Stat Block (generic / system-neutral)**
Role: Bureaucrat, witness, reluctant informant
Combat: Non-combatant. Runs if threatened.
Key competencies: Paperwork, record-keeping, forgery detection (and minor forgery), navigating bureaucratic systems.
Key weakness: Terrible under direct social pressure — a calm, reassuring approach will get more out of him than intimidation.

*System-specific note:* For CoC 7e, give him a Bureaucracy or Accounting skill around 65%, Psychology 35% (he can spot insincerity but not quickly), and Conceal around 50%. For D&D 5e, use the Commoner or Spy stat block. For GURPS 4e, Clerk at 50-75 pts with Cowardice (mild), Duty (to his employer), and Bureaucratics-14. For FitD, he has Survey 1, Consort 1, and a 2-segment clock "warn his contact."

---

**GM Notes:**

Running him at the table:
- Let him answer too fast on easy questions and too slow on the dangerous ones. That gap is the tell the players will latch onto.
- If players get frustrated and lean in hard (threats, intimidation), he shuts down completely — "I have nothing more to say, I think you should go." He's more afraid of his contact than of them.
- If they back off and come back with evidence ("we already know about the payment"), he folds quickly. He's been waiting for permission to talk.
- The moment he decides to help, his whole posture changes — the sideways angle straightens, the qualifications drop. It should feel like watching someone put down something heavy.

**The gift he's holding:** Before the session ends, decide what his piece of evidence proves. It should point toward one person or location, not solve everything — Edvard is a thread, not a confession.
