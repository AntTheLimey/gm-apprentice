## Cornelius Ashby-Vane

**Concept:** A once-respected antiquarian dealer whose years handling pre-human artefacts have fractured his sanity and transformed his encyclopaedic expertise into a liability he can barely contain.

**Appearance:** A gaunt man in his late fifties, permanently stooped as if braced for impact. His grey wool suit is fastidiously pressed but worn thin at the elbows — he can afford better but won't visit the tailor any more. He keeps his desk between himself and every door. The skin beneath his eyes is purplish, slack, the colour of old bruising.

**Portrayal:** Never faces a window. Answers questions with questions of his own for the first few exchanges, buying time to assess whether the caller is who they claim to be. When he finally commits to speech he speaks in a low, rapid monotone, as if getting the words out before he thinks better of them.

**Voice:** Drops to a near-whisper for anything that matters — the more important the information, the quieter he becomes. Refers to dangerous artefacts only by oblique labels: "the Harwich piece," "the thing from the Brompton estate." Never their descriptions. Never their names.

## AIMS Profile

**Agenda**
- *Immediate:* Establish that these particular visitors are not agents of the people (he won't say who) who have been watching his shop.
- *Short-term:* Sell or otherwise dispose of three items currently in his back room. He acquired them without understanding what they were and now will not touch them, will not describe them in writing, wants them gone before "the wrong people" trace them to him.
- *Long-term:* Liquidate his business and retire to somewhere coastal, rural, and obscure — before whatever he witnessed at the Drexler auction in 1921 catches up with him.

**Instinct:** Freeze, then flee. Under pressure his first response is to go absolutely still and silent; if that doesn't work he will find an excuse to leave the room and not come back. He will not fight and cannot be intimidated into saying more than he intends — threats make him shut down entirely.

**Moves (if PCs don't intervene)**
1. Sells one of the three back-room items at a significant loss to a collector he suspects is already compromised, just to be rid of it.
2. Begins transferring his most valuable (mundane) stock to an auction house in Edinburgh — distancing himself from his London premises.
3. Boards up the back room after the next unexplained noise from inside it and pretends it doesn't exist.

**Secrets**
- *Surface:* He is visibly nervous around new customers, glances at the door too often, keeps irregular hours posted in the window.
- *Investigation (Psychology roll or asking around in dealer circles):* He attended the Drexler estate auction three years ago, outbid everyone on a job lot described only as "continental curiosities," and returned to London looking twenty years older. He has never spoken about what was in that lot.
- *Deep (searching his private ledgers or finding his locked correspondence box):* He has been in contact with a Professor R. Culver at Miskatonic University, reporting the provenance of at least two artefacts and begging for guidance. Culver's last letter, three months old, reads only: "Destroy them if you can. If you cannot, run." The three items in the back room are from the Drexler lot. He cannot bring himself to destroy them and cannot explain why.

## The Three Back-Room Items

The Keeper should determine the exact nature of these artefacts to suit the campaign, but Ashby-Vane's description of them (if he can be persuaded to speak) is deliberately incomplete:

1. **"The bowl"** — shallow, grey-green stone, pre-dynastic at the earliest estimate. He says the incised marks on the interior are "not quite any script I've seen." He refuses to touch it without thick gloves. He hasn't examined it since the first week.
2. **"The figure"** — articulated, jointed in too many places, approximately 40 cm tall. He is not certain what material it is made from. He keeps it wrapped in two layers of canvas because "I don't like how it sits when you unwrap it."
3. **"The journal"** — bound in something dark and close-grained, pages covered in a brownish hand. He read the first three pages in 1921. He has not opened it since. He knows enough Latin and French to know this is neither.

## GM Notes

**Using Ashby-Vane:** He works best as a reluctant source — someone with exactly the knowledge the investigators need, but whose paranoia means every piece of information must be earned. He should feel like a man rationing out secrets, aware that each disclosure increases his danger.

**The key interaction loop:** He begins suspicious and cagey. A successful Psychology roll reveals that his fear is genuine, not performed — he is not hiding guilt, he is hiding terror. A successful Persuade or Charm roll (or simply demonstrating that the investigators are already in danger themselves) unlocks his willingness to share. He responds much better to "we've seen things too" than to any form of pressure.

**Escalation:** If the investigators linger too long, one of the "watchers" should make contact — perhaps a too-polite note pushed under the shop door while everyone is in the back room, or a face glimpsed at the window. This puts a clock on the scene and forces the investigators to decide how much they can extract before whoever is watching decides to act.

**If the investigators gain his full trust:** He will hand over all three artefacts and the Culver correspondence with visible relief, then promptly try to leave London. Whether they let him go is a moral decision worth giving weight to — he is not a villain, he is a man who picked up something he shouldn't have and has been paying for it ever since. Saving him (or failing to) should matter.

**SAN loss for his true nature:** None — he is human, and more victim than threat. However, if investigators examine the three artefacts, standard SAN rolls apply per the Keeper's determination of what they actually are.
