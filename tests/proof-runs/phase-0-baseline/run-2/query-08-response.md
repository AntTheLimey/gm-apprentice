# Fortune Rolls in Blades in the Dark

Fortune rolls are one of the four core roll types in Blades in the Dark (alongside action rolls, resistance rolls, and downtime rolls). Their purpose is to **disclaim GM decision-making** — when the GM would otherwise have to arbitrarily decide the outcome of something, a fortune roll lets the dice answer the question instead.

## When to Use a Fortune Roll

Fortune rolls are used when:

- **No obstacle is present but the outcome isn't common knowledge** — for example, how well a contact comes through, whether a rumour is true, or what condition an NPC is in when the crew arrives.
- **The GM wants to randomise an NPC or faction action** without bias.
- **Gathering information** where there's no meaningful obstacle but the quality of what's found is uncertain (see below).

They are explicitly *not* action rolls — no PC is rolling their own skill against a danger. The fiction doesn't place the character at risk; the GM is simply outsourcing an uncertain outcome to chance.

## Building the Dice Pool

Roll a number of d6s equal to whatever **trait rating** is most relevant to the situation:

- A faction's **Tier** (e.g. "does the Red Sashes have enough muscle to hold the district?")
- An NPC's relevant **action rating** (e.g. "does the informant successfully shake her tail?")
- A quality or magnitude rating
- A specific relevant value (e.g. crew Tier for the engagement roll and the incarceration roll)

If circumstances are favourable, add bonus dice. If unfavourable, remove them. The pool can go below 1d — if so, roll 2d and take the lowest (same rule as zero-dice action rolls; cannot crit).

## Reading the Result

| Result | Outcome |
|:---:|---------|
| Crit (two or more 6s) | Exceptional / extreme effect |
| 6 | Good / standard effect |
| 4–5 | Mixed / limited effect |
| 1–3 | Bad / poor effect |

"Effect" here is interpreted in context. For an NPC attempting something: crit means they pull it off spectacularly, 6 means clean success, 4–5 means partial or complicated, 1–3 means failure or worse.

## Common Uses in Play

### Engagement Roll (Score Opening)

The engagement roll is a fortune roll that sets the crew's **opening position** when a score begins. It starts at 1d and modifiers are added or removed based on plan quality, target strength, allies, enemies, etc.

| Result | Opening Position |
|:---:|-----------------|
| Crit | Controlled; first obstacle already overcome |
| 6 | Controlled |
| 4–5 | Risky |
| 1–3 | Desperate |

After the engagement roll, cut straight to the first serious obstacle — don't narrate the approach.

### Incarceration Roll

When a PC is caught and imprisoned, the crew rolls their **Tier** as a fortune roll to determine how the PC fares inside.

| Result | Outcome |
|:---:|---------|
| Crit | +3 rep, 1 prison claim, +1 faction status |
| 6 | 1 prison claim, +1 faction status |
| 4–5 | No incident |
| 1–3 | Suffer a level of trauma |

### Gathering Information (No Obstacle)

When PCs want to learn something and there's no meaningful obstacle in the way, the GM can call for a fortune roll to determine the **quality of information** retrieved: great effect = exceptional detail, standard = good detail, limited = partial or incomplete.

### NPC and Faction Actions

When a faction clock advances, when an NPC acts off-screen, or when the GM needs to determine how well something outside the PCs' control goes — fortune rolls provide the answer without the GM having to make an arbitrary call.

## Downtime Rolls

Downtime activity rolls (Acquire Asset, Long-Term Project, Recover, Reduce Heat) follow the **same result table** as fortune rolls (1–3 / 4–5 / 6 / crit), just applied to specific downtime effects. They're mechanically fortune rolls in a structured context.

## Key Design Point

Fortune rolls exist so the GM doesn't have to be the sole arbiter of outcomes that aren't directly tied to PC action. The dice make the world feel alive and unpredictable — even the GM doesn't always know what's going to happen next.
