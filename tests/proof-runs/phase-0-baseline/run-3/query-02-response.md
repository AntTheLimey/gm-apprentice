## Reginald Pugh Ashworth

**Concept:** A once-celebrated antiquarian who handled an object he should never have touched, and has spent the years since dismantling his own life to prevent anyone else from finding it.

**Appearance:** Mid-fifties, gaunt where he used to be merely lean. His suits were expensive once; now they are carefully mended, the lapels slightly wrong for the current decade. He keeps his hands in his pockets whenever he can — not from cold, but because he dislikes people looking at his fingers, which bear old burn scars in an arrangement he has never explained.

**Portrayal:** Speaks at two volumes: a flat professional murmur for anything he doesn't care about, and a rapid, slightly too-loud intensity for anything he does. Trails off mid-sentence when certain topics come near. Asks questions before answering any. Has a habit of repositioning whatever is nearest to him — a pen, a cup, a paperweight — until it sits at an angle he apparently finds acceptable.

**Voice:** Starts sentences with qualifications — "That depends entirely on what you mean by..." or "I'd need to know more before I could say..." Never answers a direct question directly. When genuinely frightened, becomes precise and formal, as though writing a report to himself.

## AIMS Profile

**Agenda**
- *Immediate:* Determine whether these investigators are what they claim to be, or whether someone sent them. Reveal nothing useful until he is certain.
- *Short-term:* Locate and destroy — or permanently relocate beyond reach — a specific artefact he sold in 1921 to a buyer whose name he has since tried to forget. He believes the buyer is dead. He is not entirely certain.
- *Long-term:* Die without having passed his knowledge on to anyone who might use it. He has drafted a will that leaves his entire library to a municipal incinerator. He has not yet signed it.

**Instinct:** Freeze, then flee. Under real pressure his first impulse is stillness — he has learned to look ordinary while his mind races. If cornered past that point he runs, makes excuses, manufactures emergencies. He does not fight; he is not built for it and knows it.

**Moves (if PCs don't intervene)**
1. Within the week he makes quiet enquiries about the investigators — who recommended them, who they have spoken to, whether their names appear in certain circles. He has a contact at a local newspaper who owes him a favour.
2. If his enquiries turn up anything alarming, he begins quietly liquidating stock and forwarding the proceeds to an account under a different name. He has done this before.
3. If he becomes convinced the artefact is moving again — changing hands, surfacing at auction — he will make contact with the original buyer's estate, regardless of the risk to himself.

**Secrets**
- *Surface:* Jumpy around unexpected visitors. Keeps his shop uncomfortably cold, even in summer. The hours posted on the door are fictional.
- *Investigation (Library Use or Psychology, Hard):* He closed his London premises in 1923 after a significant inventory loss he logged as fire damage. Insurance records show no fire. Three items listed as destroyed appear in private collection catalogues from 1924 onward. He sold them quietly, at a loss, to buyers he believed were nowhere near the problem.
- *Deep (substantial research or breaking his confidence):* In 1921 he acquired a pre-Columbian obsidian mirror from a Peruvian collection. While cleaning it, he saw something looking back. He does not know what it was. He does not know if it knew what it was looking at. He does not know if it is still there. He sold the mirror the following month to a collector named Elias Croft, and has since learned that Croft's estate was dispersed at auction in 1926 after Croft died under circumstances the coroner described as "cardiac arrest secondary to acute nervous collapse." The mirror is unaccounted for. Ashworth's Cthulhu Mythos skill is 12% — enough to understand that what he saw was not his imagination, not enough to understand anything else.

## Relationships

- **Elias Croft (deceased):** The buyer. Ashworth has been quietly collecting every record of Croft's collection dispersal he can find. He has not located the mirror.
- **Miss Dorothea Crane, rare manuscripts dealer (London):** The one colleague he trusts. She does not know why he left London. He would do a great deal to keep her from finding out.
- **An unnamed academic (university position, field unspecified):** Has contacted Ashworth twice in the past year asking about pre-Columbian obsidian artefacts. Ashworth has not replied. He does not know whether this is coincidence.
- **The investigators:** Unknown quantity. He is watching them carefully.

## GM Notes

**Running Ashworth:** He is not a villain and not a victim — he is a man who made one ordinary professional mistake and has been living with the consequences ever since. The paranoia is earned. Treat it as intelligence, not comedy. He should feel like someone who has almost figured out how to be safe.

**What he can offer:** He is a world-class resource on artefact provenance, antiquities networks, and the grey market for unusual objects. If the investigators gain his trust — genuinely, not through manipulation — he becomes extremely useful. He knows names. He knows where things have been. He knows which auction houses ask the wrong questions and which ones never ask any.

**What makes him break:** Evidence that the mirror has surfaced, or that Croft's death was not natural causes. Either will override his caution. He has spent three years being careful, and he is tired of it.

**Sanity cost if his true nature is revealed:** If Mythos knowledge is confirmed to him — if investigators show him proof that what he saw was real — he loses 1D4 SAN. He was already fairly certain. Certainty is worse.

**Hooks:**
- He is the only person who handled the artefact before sale and survived intact. He is therefore the only living witness to its properties.
- His systematic dismantling of his own professional network means there are provenance gaps in the historical record that investigators may need to fill.
- He has a ledger he has not shown anyone, kept in a locked drawer behind a false back. It lists every object he has sold since 1921 that he considered potentially dangerous, with buyer names and last known locations.
