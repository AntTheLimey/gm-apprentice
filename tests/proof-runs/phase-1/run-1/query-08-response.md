## Fortune Rolls in Blades in the Dark

A Fortune Roll is the GM's tool for resolving uncertain outcomes that don't belong to a specific PC's action — or when you want to take your hands off the wheel and let the dice decide. Rather than the GM simply declaring what happens, a Fortune Roll delegates the decision to chance.

### Core Purpose

Fortune Rolls are used to **disclaim decision-making** in three main situations:

1. **Gathering information with no obstacle** — if the answer isn't common knowledge but there's nothing actively resisting the PCs finding out, a fortune roll determines quality of the information rather than an action roll.
2. **Engagement Roll** — the fortune roll that sets the opening position of a score (see below).
3. **Off-screen events** — resolving what NPCs, factions, or the world is doing when the PCs aren't directly involved (e.g., "does the rival gang hit the target before us?").

### How to Roll

Assemble a pool of d6s based on the relevant trait, rating, or factor — there's no fixed attribute; you use whatever is most appropriate to the situation (a faction's Tier, an NPC's quality rating, a relevant action rating, etc.).

Roll the pool and read the **single highest die**:

| Result | Outcome |
|:------:|---------|
| Crit (two or more 6s) | Exceptional / extreme effect |
| 6 | Good / standard effect |
| 4-5 | Mixed / limited effect |
| 1-3 | Bad / poor effect |

If you have **zero dice**, roll 2d6 and take the lower result (and you cannot crit).

### The Engagement Roll

The most structured Fortune Roll in the game is the **Engagement Roll**, which opens every score. It starts at 1d and is modified by plan quality and circumstances:

| Factor | Modifier |
|--------|:--------:|
| Bold or daring plan | +1d |
| Overly complex or contingent | -1d |
| Plan detail exposes a vulnerability | +1d |
| Target is strongest against this approach | -1d |
| Friends or contacts aid the approach | +1d |
| Enemies or rivals interfere | -1d |
| Other advantage (e.g., lower-Tier target) | +1d |
| Other disadvantage (e.g., higher-Tier target) | -1d |

The result sets the **opening position** of the score:

| Result | Opening Position |
|:------:|-----------------|
| Crit | Controlled; first obstacle already overcome |
| 6 | Controlled |
| 4-5 | Risky |
| 1-3 | Desperate |

After this roll, cut immediately to the first serious obstacle — don't narrate the approach. The engagement position applies to the first roll only; subsequent rolls use normal position rules.

### Fortune Rolls for Information

When the PCs want to know something and there's no active obstacle blocking them (not common knowledge, but not guarded either), use a Fortune Roll instead of an action roll. The **effect level determines how much detail they get**:

- **Great effect** — exceptional detail, full picture
- **Standard effect** — solid, useful information
- **Limited effect** — partial or incomplete

For complex questions that would take sustained effort to answer, use a **long-term project clock** during downtime instead.

### Downtime Rolls

Downtime activities (Acquire Asset, Long-Term Project, Recover, Reduce Heat) use the same result progression as Fortune Rolls:

| Result | Effect |
|:------:|--------|
| Crit | 5 ticks / Tier+2 asset |
| 6 | 3 ticks / Tier+1 asset |
| 4-5 | 2 ticks / Tier asset |
| 1-3 | 1 tick / Tier-1 asset |

These are technically distinct (called Downtime Rolls) but use identical mechanics.

### Key Differences from Action Rolls

| | Action Roll | Fortune Roll |
|---|---|---|
| Who rolls | A PC | GM, or GM delegates to a player |
| Position/Effect | Set by GM before rolling | Not applicable |
| Consequences | Yes — 4-5 and 1-3 carry costs | No — the result IS the outcome |
| Stress/push/assist | Available | Not applicable |
| Purpose | Resolving a PC's risky attempt | Resolving world events, NPC actions, information quality |

### GM Tips

- Use Fortune Rolls liberally to resolve off-screen threats, NPC contests, or anything the GM shouldn't just decide unilaterally.
- When a score's engagement roll goes badly (1-3 → desperate), flashbacks are the natural response — players can retroactively establish preparations they made "just in case."
- You can use a faction's Tier as the die pool for faction-vs-faction fortune rolls (e.g., "do the Bluecoats catch the Lampblacks before the crew can intervene?").
