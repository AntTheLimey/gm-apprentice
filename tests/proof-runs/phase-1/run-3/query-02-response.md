## Cornelius Vane

**Concept:** A retired antiquarian dealer whose three decades handling cursed objects and occult manuscripts have left him convinced — correctly — that something hunts the collectors who know too much.

**Appearance:** A stooped man in his late sixties with the jerky economy of movement of someone who has learned to never turn his back on a doorway. His clothes are expensive but worn: a good wool suit gone shiny at the elbows, waistcoat buttoned to the throat regardless of season. He keeps his hands clasped together when sitting, as though restraining them. His office smells of camphor, old vellum, and something chemical he uses to clean things he refuses to name. Every mirror in his shop is covered with black cloth.

**Portrayal:** Vane speaks in asides and qualifications — he answers every question by first answering a different question he considers safer. He never faces a window and insists on sitting with his back to the wall. When something surprises him, he freezes completely for two or three seconds before responding, as if running a private diagnostic. He uses elaborate formality as armour: calling investigators "my dear fellow" or "my dear lady" with the same courteous distance whether he's giving directions or telling someone they are going to die.

**Voice:** Formal diction, circuitous approach. Speaks in dependent clauses that delay the main point by one sentence longer than comfortable. "One might, if one were inclined toward such observations — and there is, I confess, some reason to be — suggest that the provenance of that particular piece is not the sort one discusses in a room with open windows."

### AIMS Profile

**Agenda:**
- *Immediate:* Assess whether investigators are the sort of people who will get themselves killed or the sort who might, improbably, not.
- *Short-term:* Sell or transfer a particular item that has come into his possession and which he cannot safely destroy. He wants it out of his shop within the fortnight.
- *Long-term:* Live out his remaining years without attracting the attention of whatever took his former business partner, Hargreaves, in 1921.

**Instinct:** Freeze then flee. Under genuine pressure, Vane goes very still and very quiet, assesses the available exits, and then either runs or (if cornered) deploys his one defensive resource — a revolver he has carried for eleven years and never fired at anything human.

**Moves (on a timeline if PCs don't intervene):**
1. *Within three days:* Places an oblique advertisement in a specialist journal for a buyer of "scholarly artefacts of uncertain classification." Written to be incomprehensible to anyone without specific knowledge of occult collector circles.
2. *Within ten days:* If no buyer materialises, he will attempt to ship the item anonymously to the British Museum's incoming donations desk, wrapped in newspaper, with no return address and a note saying only "Destroy this. You won't."
3. *Within fourteen days:* If the item remains and he believes he is being watched, he will close the shop without explanation, move to a rented room under a false name, and begin quietly making arrangements to leave the country.

**Secrets:**
- *Surface:* He refuses to discuss a specific shelf in the back room, always locked. He has covered every reflective surface in the building. He checks the street from behind the curtain before opening the door.
- *Investigation (Psychology roll or direct questioning once trust established):* Hargreaves — his business partner of fifteen years — disappeared overnight in November 1921, three days after they authenticated a particular bronze figurine. Vane found the shop empty in the morning. Not ransacked. The figurine was still there. Hargreaves was not.
- *Deep (Library Use + successful Persuade, or access to his private journals):* Vane has a Cthulhu Mythos 14%. He has read three partial tomes over the course of his career, and has drawn conclusions that are correct, specific, and which he will not commit to paper. He knows — not believes, *knows* — that the figurine he currently holds is a locus object, that it draws attention from something he calls "the patient watchers," and that Hargreaves was not taken but *replaced.* He encountered the replacement twice before it stopped coming to the shop. He does not know where it is now. He checks faces very carefully.

---

### Relationship Web

- **Hargreaves (the original):** Missing since 1921. Vane's closest friend and professional partner for fifteen years. Vane privately believes he is dead and that grief, not just fear, is what keeps him in the shop — leaving feels like abandonment.
- **Inspector Wellesley, Metropolitan Police (retired):** An old acquaintance who once investigated a related disappearance and filed it as misadventure. Vane has not contacted him since. He could be reactivated as a resource but Vane considers it likely to get Wellesley killed.
- **The Hargreaves Replacement:** An entity using Hargreaves' appearance and general mannerisms. Vane is the only living person who knows the difference. It has not appeared for eighteen months. He does not consider this reassuring.
- **A network of specialist dealers and collectors:** Several correspondents who share Vane's interests in occult provenance. Two have died in unusual circumstances in the past decade. He continues the correspondence but no longer tells them anything useful.

### GM Notes

**Running Vane:** The key tension is that he is both a resource and a liability. He has genuinely useful information — he is one of the few people who understands, at a practical level, what certain Mythos objects actually do. But extracting it requires demonstrating to his satisfaction that the investigators are competent enough to survive being told the truth. He gatekeeps information not from malice but from a sincere and evidence-based belief that knowing has a price and most people are not equipped to pay it.

His paranoia should read as *earned*, not pathological. Every observable tic — the covered mirrors, the back-to-the-wall seating, the identity-verification questions — has a concrete rationale. When PCs uncover the rationale, they should feel the chill of realising he is not delusional: he is simply responding correctly to an environment most people cannot perceive.

**The item in his possession** is deliberately left open. It could be the original figurine from 1921 (how did he end up with it again?), or something else entirely that has arrived through normal trade channels and which he has since identified as similar in kind. Keepers should decide whether the figurine plotline is active or historical.

**SAN loss on true nature disclosure:** If investigators determine that the Hargreaves replacement exists and is still active, Keeper's discretion — suggest 1/1D6 SAN for the confirmation that identity-replacement is happening in their city.

**Clue thread:** Vane's encoded journal, if cracked, contains dates, object descriptions, and partial conclusions that could serve as a significant research accelerant for a Mythos investigation — and would constitute roughly a 1D3 Cthulhu Mythos gain to read in full.
