# CoC 7e Chase Rules

Source: `skills/ttrpg-expert/systems/coc-7e/rules-reference.md` (Chase Rules section).

---

## Overview

CoC 7e uses a structured, abstract chase system built on a point-to-point track. It replaces free-form pursuit with a sequence of move, hazard, and action rolls that keeps the drama tight and gives both sides meaningful choices each round.

---

## Setting Up the Chase

1. **Draw a chase track.** Create a line of 10–20 abstract spaces representing the pursuit route (a street, a corridor, a rooftop sequence, etc.).
2. **Place participants.** Put the pursuer and the quarry at their starting positions on the track. A head-start can be represented by placing the quarry several spaces ahead.
3. **Place hazards.** The Keeper scatters hazards at intervals — fences, traffic, locked doors, crowds, obstacles relevant to the environment. Hazards create skill-check opportunities and slow characters down.

---

## Chase Round (repeating until resolved)

Each round proceeds as follows:

1. **Move.** Each participant moves a number of spaces equal to their **MOV** score.
   - Standard human MOV is 7–9 depending on build (see character sheet).
   - Creatures may have higher or lower MOV.

2. **Optional sprint.** A character may attempt a **CON roll** to gain **+1 space** of movement.
   - Success: +1 space.
   - Failure: no bonus, and the character risks fatigue (Keeper's discretion).

3. **Hazards.** If a character enters a space containing a hazard, they must roll the appropriate skill to navigate it (Athletics for a fence, Drive for a vehicle hazard, etc.).
   - Failure: lose **1 space** and possibly take damage or incur another consequence.

4. **Actions.** On their turn, participants may spend their action on:
   - **Attack** (melee or ranged, if in range).
   - **Skill use** (e.g., calling for help, hot-wiring a car).
   - **Creating obstacles** — placing something in the path of those behind them, which functions as a new hazard.

---

## Ending the Chase

| Outcome | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Caught** | The pursuer occupies the same space as the quarry. |
| **Escaped** | The quarry reaches the end of the track, or opens a gap too large for the pursuer to close in any reasonable number of rounds. |
| **Abandoned** | The pursuer chooses to stop, or is incapacitated by a hazard or injury. |

### Complications

Chases through populated or sensitive areas may generate secondary consequences regardless of who wins:

- Bystander injuries (potential SAN loss or legal trouble).
- Property damage (police attention, enemies alerted).
- Attracting witnesses or law enforcement.

The Keeper should roll these in as they make dramatic sense rather than mechanically mandating them every chase.

---

## Key Skills and Stats in a Chase

| Stat / Skill | Role |
|-------------|------|
| **MOV** | Primary movement; determines spaces per round |
| **CON** | Sprint roll to gain +1 space |
| **Athletics** | Navigate physical hazards (fences, walls, rough terrain) |
| **Drive / Pilot** | Navigate vehicle hazards |
| **Dodge** | Defensive reaction if attacked during the chase |
| **Fighting / Firearms** | Attack rolls against other participants |

---

## Keeper Tips

- **Keep the track visual.** Even a rough sketch on paper prevents confusion about who is where and where the next hazard is.
- **Hazards should fit the fiction.** A chase through a Victorian market has different hazards than one through a speakeasy basement or a foggy harbour.
- **MOV differences matter.** A creature with MOV 12 chasing investigators with MOV 8 closes 4 spaces per round — with no hazards, a 10-space track gives investigators only 2–3 rounds to act. Adjust track length or hazard density to match the intended tension.
- **The optional CON sprint is risky.** Encourage players to use it, then let the fatigue cost bite — it raises stakes without being punishing.
- **Failed hazard rolls are story moments.** A fence that trips the investigator, a car that clips them — these are fail-forward opportunities, not dead stops.
