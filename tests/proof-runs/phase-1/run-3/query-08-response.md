# Fortune Rolls in Blades in the Dark

A **fortune roll** is a special roll the GM makes (or calls for) to disclaim a decision to the dice when no PC action is directly involved. It answers the question "how does this turn out?" without putting a character's action rating at stake.

## The Basic Mechanic

Build a dice pool from a relevant trait rating — a faction's Tier, an NPC's quality, a situation's inherent potency, or simply 1d as a baseline — then add modifiers for advantages and disadvantages. Roll and read the **single highest die**:

| Result | Outcome |
|:---:|---------|
| Critical (two+ 6s) | Exceptional / extreme effect |
| 6 | Good outcome / standard effect |
| 4–5 | Mixed result / limited effect |
| 1–3 | Bad outcome / poor effect |

At zero dice (no applicable trait), roll 2d6 and take the **lowest** — you cannot crit at zero.

## When to Use a Fortune Roll

Fortune rolls replace GM fiat in situations where a fair, unbiased outcome is needed:

- **Gathering information without an obstacle** — if there is no danger or opposition and the information is not common knowledge, a fortune roll determines quality (great = exceptional detail, limited = partial clues).
- **Engagement roll** — this is a fortune roll starting from 1d, modified by plan boldness, target strength, and outside aid. It sets the opening position for the score (crit/6 = controlled, 4–5 = risky, 1–3 = desperate).
- **NPC or faction activity** — when a faction takes an action off-screen, roll their Tier (or quality) to see how well it goes.
- **Downtime rolls** — acquiring assets, long-term projects, recovery, and heat reduction all use the same dice progression as a fortune roll (same result table, same reading).
- **Resolving background fiction** — any "what happens to X while the crew is away?" question the GM does not want to answer by fiat.

## Building the Dice Pool

Start from the most relevant trait:
- **NPC/faction quality or Tier** for off-screen events
- **1d baseline** for uncertain world events with no clear rating
- **Action rating** if an NPC is taking an action
- **Attribute rating** for broad capability checks

Add +1d per meaningful advantage (allies, superior position, preparation) and subtract dice per disadvantage. The pool has no upper cap, but cannot go below 0d (roll 2d take lowest).

## Fortune Roll vs. Action Roll

The key distinction:

| Fortune Roll | Action Roll |
|-------------|-------------|
| GM rolls, or called by GM | Player rolls |
| No position/effect; no consequences to the PC | Position + effect + consequences |
| Disclaims GM decision | Resolves PC attempt at dangerous/troublesome goal |
| No stress, no harm, no resistance | Can trigger harm, resistance, push |

A fortune roll is **not** used when a PC is doing something — that is always an action roll. Fortune rolls are for the world acting, NPC activity, or information quality when no obstacle is present.

## Engagement Roll (Fortune Roll in Practice)

The most common structured fortune roll is the **engagement roll** at the start of a score. It starts at 1d and is modified as follows:

| Factor | Modifier |
|--------|:---:|
| Bold or daring plan | +1d |
| Overly complex or contingent plan | -1d |
| Detail exposes a vulnerability | +1d |
| Target's strength matches this approach | -1d |
| Friends or contacts aid the approach | +1d |
| Enemies or rivals interfere | -1d |
| Other advantage (e.g. lower-Tier target) | +1d |
| Other disadvantage (e.g. higher-Tier target) | -1d |

The result sets the **opening position** of the score — it does not narrate the approach, it just tells you where the crew lands when the first serious obstacle appears.

## Downtime Rolls (Same Progression)

Downtime activities use the same result ladder:

| Result | Ticks / Effect |
|:---:|----------------|
| Critical | 5 ticks (project/healing); exceptional asset |
| 6 | 3 ticks; asset at Tier +1 |
| 4–5 | 2 ticks; asset at Tier |
| 1–3 | 1 tick; asset at Tier -1 |

Spending **1 coin after the roll** bumps the result up one level. A friend or contact helping the activity adds +1d.

## GM Notes

- Use fortune rolls liberally to keep the world feeling alive and impartial — they signal that outcomes are not predetermined.
- Do not call for a fortune roll when a PC is the agent; that collapses into an action roll.
- When a faction clock advances between sessions, you can use a fortune roll against the faction's Tier to determine whether they overcame an obstacle or ran into unexpected trouble.
- Fortune rolls have no "consequence" structure — no harm, no resistance, no position. They simply answer the question asked.
