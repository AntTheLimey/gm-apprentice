## Fortune Rolls in Blades in the Dark

A Fortune Roll is one of the four core roll types in Blades in the Dark (alongside Action Rolls, Resistance Rolls, and Downtime Rolls). Its purpose is to **disclaim GM decision-making** — when the outcome of a situation should be determined by chance or by a character/faction's inherent capability rather than by a PC actively attempting something, you use a Fortune Roll.

### How to Make a Fortune Roll

1. Identify the relevant **trait** — this could be an action rating, an NPC's quality rating, a crew's Tier, or any other applicable numeric trait. If there's no clear trait, roll 1d (or 2d and take the lowest for a particularly weak position).
2. Assemble your dice pool from that trait rating, plus any relevant bonuses.
3. Roll and read the **single highest die**.

### Results

| Result | Outcome |
|:---:|---------|
| Crit (two or more 6s) | Exceptional / extreme effect |
| 6 | Good / standard effect |
| 4-5 | Mixed / limited effect |
| 1-3 | Bad / poor effect |

### When to Use a Fortune Roll

Fortune Rolls cover any situation where the GM wants randomness to decide rather than player agency. Common uses include:

**Gathering Information (no obstacle present):** If the PCs want to know something and there's no active obstacle blocking them, but the information isn't common knowledge, use a Fortune Roll to determine the quality of what they learn. Effect level maps to detail: great effect = exceptional detail, standard = good detail, limited = partial or incomplete.

**The Engagement Roll:** At the start of every score, the GM makes a Fortune Roll (starting from 1d, modified by plan quality, advantages, and disadvantages) to set the crew's opening position. This determines whether they begin in a controlled, risky, or desperate state.

**Off-Screen Events:** When factions act, clocks advance without PC involvement, or NPCs pursue goals, Fortune Rolls resolve the outcome without requiring an Action Roll.

**NPC vs. NPC conflicts:** When two NPCs or factions are in contest and the PCs aren't directly involved, roll appropriate trait ratings for each and compare, or simply roll one side's trait to see how well they fare.

**Downtime Activities:** Downtime Rolls use the same progression as Fortune Rolls (1-3/4-5/6/crit). Actions like Acquire Asset, Recover, and Reduce Heat all follow this same result table.

### Dice Pool Construction

The trait you roll is usually:
- An **action rating** (0-4 dots) for a specific character
- A **quality/Tier rating** for a gang, crew, or resource
- A **single die (1d)** as a baseline for uncertain situations with no clear trait

Modifiers stack normally. A Fortune Roll can also be made with zero dice (roll 2d, take the lowest; cannot crit).

### Key Distinction from Action Rolls

Unlike Action Rolls, Fortune Rolls have **no position or effect pre-set** — the result table directly maps to outcome quality. There's also no stress cost, no resistance option built in, and no player agency involved. The GM calls for the roll, reads the result, and applies it to the fiction. Players don't choose which action to use and can't push themselves or accept Devil's Bargains on Fortune Rolls (those mechanics apply only to Action Rolls).

### Example

The crew's street-level contact is trying to find a hidden safehouse before the Bluecoats do. The GM rolls the contact's quality (Tier II, so 2d) as a Fortune Roll. Result: a 5. Mixed/limited effect — the contact finds a lead but arrives just after the Bluecoats sweep the area. The crew has partial information but is now behind the clock.
