# Session Planner: PC-Focused Prep and Character Arc Management

This document provides structured workflows for planning
sessions that give every player character dedicated content,
track and advance individual character arcs across a campaign,
and balance spotlight time equitably across the table.

Read this file whenever preparing a session, planning a
campaign arc, or when the user asks for help ensuring each
PC gets meaningful content in an upcoming session.

## Philosophy: Characters Are the Story

The single highest-value prep activity is reviewing the player
characters and asking: what can this session do for each of
them? Players invest deeply in their characters. Sessions that
treat PCs as interchangeable adventurers waste that investment.
Sessions that make each PC the hero of their own thread within
the larger story create the memorable campaigns people talk
about for years.

This principle comes from Sly Flourish's "Step Zero" framework,
where reviewing the characters is the first thing a GM does
before any other prep — before scenes, before NPCs, before
encounters. Everything else flows from the characters.

Reference: Sly Flourish, Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master.

## The PC Roster Review

Before planning any session, complete a roster review for every
active PC. This is the foundation of character-focused prep.

### PC Roster Review Template

For each PC, answer:

```
PC NAME: _______________
PLAYER: _______________

BACKSTORY HOOKS (unresolved elements from character history):
- [List backstory elements not yet addressed in play]

CURRENT GOALS (what the PC is actively pursuing):
- [List stated goals, both short-term and long-term]

ACTIVE ARC STAGE (see Arc Stages below):
- [Current arc phase and what the next beat should be]

RELATIONSHIPS (NPCs and PCs this character cares about):
- [List key relationships and their current state]

RECENT DECISIONS (choices made in last 1-3 sessions):
- [List decisions that should have consequences]

MECHANICAL HIGHLIGHTS (abilities that haven't been showcased):
- [List skills, spells, features, or equipment waiting for
  a moment to shine]

LAST SPOTLIGHT SESSION: _______________
SESSIONS SINCE LAST SPOTLIGHT: _______________

THIS SESSION'S TOUCHPOINT:
- [Plan at least one moment specifically for this PC]
```

Complete this for every PC before planning scenes. The
touchpoint at the bottom is the critical output — it ensures
no PC is forgotten during prep.

## Character Arc Stages

Character arcs in TTRPGs follow a structure similar to
television series arcs, where each character has a personal
storyline that weaves through the ensemble narrative. Track
each PC's arc using these stages.

### The Five-Stage Arc Model

**Stage 1: Establishment**

The character's situation, beliefs, flaws, and goals are
introduced. The player defines who this person is. The GM
notes what elements have dramatic potential.

GM actions during Establishment:
- Record the PC's stated beliefs, values, and goals.
- Identify which backstory elements can connect to the
  campaign's main arc.
- Introduce NPCs who reflect, challenge, or depend on the
  PC's stated identity.
- Plant seeds: mention names, places, or events from the
  PC's history in the world.

Typical duration: Sessions 1-3 of a campaign or arc.

**Stage 2: Testing**

The campaign presents situations that test the character's
beliefs, strain their relationships, or complicate their
goals. The PC faces choices that reveal who they really are
under pressure.

GM actions during Testing:
- Design encounters that specifically probe this PC's stated
  values (a pacifist character encounters a situation where
  violence seems like the only answer).
- Introduce moral dilemmas with no clean solution that touch
  this PC's core beliefs.
- Create NPCs who hold opposing but sympathetic viewpoints.
- Present opportunities that tempt the PC away from their
  stated goals.

Typical duration: The longest phase. Sessions 3-10+ depending
on campaign length.

**Stage 3: Crisis**

A turning point forces the character to confront their flaw,
make a defining choice, or face the consequences of their
past. This is the PC's "big moment" — the scene the player
will remember.

GM actions during Crisis:
- Build toward this moment deliberately. The player should
  feel the pressure mounting over 2-3 sessions before it
  arrives.
- Make the stakes personal and irreversible. This choice
  should change the character or the world permanently.
- Ensure the rest of the party witnesses or participates.
  A crisis that happens offscreen wastes its impact.
- Do not predetermine the outcome. The player must choose.

Typical duration: 1-2 sessions for the crisis itself,
preceded by 2-3 sessions of escalation.

**Stage 4: Transformation**

The character changes as a result of the crisis. They abandon
an old belief, adopt a new one, achieve their goal and find
it hollow, lose something precious, or grow into a new
version of themselves.

GM actions during Transformation:
- Reflect the change back to the player through NPC reactions,
  world consequences, and new opportunities.
- Allow the player to update their character's stated beliefs,
  goals, or personality.
- Introduce new complications or goals that arise from the
  transformation.
- Create moments where other PCs react to the change.

Typical duration: 1-3 sessions.

**Stage 5: New Equilibrium**

The character settles into their changed state. This becomes
the new Establishment for the next arc. Some characters cycle
through multiple arcs in a long campaign; others reach a
satisfying conclusion.

GM actions during New Equilibrium:
- Acknowledge the completed arc. Let the player enjoy the
  resolution before introducing new pressure.
- Plant seeds for the next arc if the campaign continues.
- Use this character's completed arc as connective tissue
  for other PCs' ongoing arcs (the transformed character
  can now serve as mentor, cautionary tale, or catalyst
  for another PC's crisis).

Typical duration: 1-2 sessions before the next arc begins.

### Arc Tracker Template

Maintain a tracker for all PCs:

```
CAMPAIGN: _______________
CURRENT SESSION: _______________

| PC | Arc Theme | Stage | Next Beat | Target Session |
|----|-----------|-------|-----------|----------------|
|    |           |       |           |                |
|    |           |       |           |                |
|    |           |       |           |                |
|    |           |       |           |                |
```

**Arc Theme** is a short phrase describing the arc's core
tension (e.g. "loyalty vs. freedom", "confronting the past",
"proving worthy", "choosing between duty and love").

**Next Beat** is the specific scene or event that advances
this arc one step.

**Target Session** is when you plan to deliver the next beat.
Stagger these across sessions so the campaign does not try
to advance every arc simultaneously.

## Spotlight Planning

### The A/B/C Plot Model

Borrow the television writers' room concept of parallel
storylines running at different intensities.

**A-Plot:** The main campaign storyline. All PCs are involved.
This is the primary arc for the session. Typically occupies
50-60% of session time.

**B-Plot:** A personal arc for one specific PC. This is the
"featured player" for the session. The B-plot gives this PC
dedicated content — an NPC from their past appears, a moral
dilemma targets their values, a scene is built around their
abilities. Typically occupies 25-35% of session time.

**C-Plot:** A lighter personal touch for a second PC. A brief
scene, a callback, a clue related to their arc. Not a full
spotlight but enough to signal that their story is advancing.
Typically occupies 10-15% of session time.

### B-Plot Rotation

Rotate which PC gets the B-plot each session. Track the
rotation explicitly:

```
| Session | A-Plot | B-Plot (Featured PC) | C-Plot (Secondary PC) |
|---------|--------|----------------------|-----------------------|
| 12      | Cult   | Elara (mentor return)| Thane (duty letter)   |
| 13      | Cult   | Thane (duty crisis)  | Mira (vision clue)    |
| 14      | Cult   | Mira (vision payoff) | Kaelen (rival sighted)|
| 15      | Cult   | Kaelen (rival duel)  | Elara (mentor fallout)|
```

Rules for the rotation:
- Every PC gets a B-plot within N sessions, where N equals
  the number of PCs.
- A PC in crisis (Arc Stage 3) may get consecutive B-plots
  to maintain momentum.
- If a PC had the B-plot last session, they should not have
  it again this session unless they are mid-crisis.
- The C-plot PC from session N often becomes the B-plot PC
  for session N+1 or N+2, creating a sense of building
  momentum.

### Spotlight Tracking

After each session, record how spotlight time was distributed.
This does not need to be precise — a rough estimate is enough
to catch patterns.

```
SESSION: _______________

| PC | Spotlight Share | Notes |
|----|----------------|-------|
|    | ___% | |
|    | ___% | |
|    | ___% | |
|    | ___% | |

IMBALANCE FLAG: [Yes/No]
If yes, corrective action for next session: _______________
```

A session where one PC gets less than 15% of spotlight time
(in a 4-player party) should trigger a corrective plan for
the next session. Sustained imbalance over 3+ sessions is a
serious problem.

### Weaving Personal Content into Group Scenes

The best spotlight moments happen when the whole party is
present. Splitting the party for personal scenes burns group
time and risks disengaging the other players. Instead:

**Embed personal content in group encounters.** If the B-plot
PC is a former soldier haunted by their past, the group's
visit to a military outpost becomes the vehicle for that
personal content. Everyone is in the scene; the personal
stakes emerge naturally.

**Use NPCs as vectors.** An NPC connected to one PC's
backstory appears in a scene that involves the whole party.
The party meets the NPC for plot reasons; the personal
connection is a bonus layer.

**Create multi-hook encounters.** Design encounters with
multiple elements that appeal to different PCs. A social
encounter at a noble's ball might feature: an informant the
rogue needs (PC 1), the noble who owes the paladin's family
a debt (PC 2), a rare book the wizard is seeking (PC 3),
and a hidden shrine the cleric can sense (PC 4).

**Let other PCs witness and react.** A personal moment gains
weight when other characters are present to react. The party
seeing the stoic fighter break down upon recognising their
lost sibling is far more powerful than it happening in a
solo scene.

## PC Touchpoint Design

A "touchpoint" is any content element in a session that is
specifically designed for one PC. Every session should contain
at least one touchpoint per PC. Not all touchpoints need to
be major scenes — some are brief moments that signal the
campaign remembers and cares about each character.

### Touchpoint Types

**Backstory Connections (High Impact)**

An NPC, location, or event from the PC's history appears in
the current session. This creates the feeling that the
character's past is alive in the world.

Examples:
- A letter arrives from the PC's hometown.
- An NPC recognises the PC from a shared past event.
- The party visits a location the PC described in their
  backstory.
- An enemy connected to the PC's origin makes a move.

Design note: Use backstory connections sparingly — 1-2 per
arc, not every session. Overuse makes the world feel
artificially centred on the PCs. Underuse wastes rich
material the player provided.

**Moral Dilemmas (High Impact)**

A situation forces the PC to choose between competing values.
The best dilemmas target the specific tensions in a PC's
stated beliefs.

Examples:
- The honourable knight must choose between obeying an
  unjust order or defying legitimate authority.
- The scholar must decide whether forbidden knowledge is
  worth the cost of obtaining it.
- The loyal friend must choose between the party's mission
  and a personal obligation.

Design note: Moral dilemmas should have no clean answer.
If the "right" choice is obvious, it is not a dilemma.

**Ability Showcases (Medium Impact)**

An encounter is designed so that one PC's specific skills,
spells, or abilities are the key to success. This makes the
player feel their character choices matter mechanically.

Examples:
- A locked mechanism that requires the rogue's specific
  toolkit.
- A magical ward that the wizard's school of magic is
  uniquely suited to counter.
- A social encounter where the bard's specific language
  or contact is essential.
- A physical challenge that demands the barbarian's raw
  strength.

Design note: Rotate these so every PC gets mechanical
spotlight. Avoid repeatedly showcasing the same character's
abilities.

**Decision Callbacks (Medium Impact)**

The world reacts to a choice the PC made in a previous
session. This is one of the most powerful tools for making
players feel their agency matters.

Examples:
- The bandit the PC spared reappears — as an ally or as a
  more dangerous enemy.
- A village the party saved sends a gift or a plea for help.
- An NPC the PC lied to has discovered the truth.
- The faction the PC offended takes action.

Design note: Track PC decisions in the post-session recap
(see active-play-management.md) and schedule callbacks 2-5
sessions later. Immediate callbacks feel reactive; delayed
callbacks feel like the world has memory.

**Arc Advancement Clues (Medium Impact)**

A clue, hint, or piece of information that advances the PC's
personal arc. This signals that their storyline is progressing
even in sessions where they are not the B-plot.

Examples:
- A rumour related to the PC's long-lost family member.
- A document mentioning the organisation the PC is
  investigating.
- A dream or vision foreshadowing the next arc beat.
- An NPC casually mentions something relevant to the PC's
  quest.

Design note: These are ideal C-plot touchpoints. Brief,
suggestive, and forward-looking.

**Character Moments (Low-Medium Impact)**

A quiet scene that lets the PC express who they are without
plot pressure. Campfire conversations, downtime activities,
reactions to events. These are where players often do their
best roleplaying.

Examples:
- The GM asks "What does [PC name] do during the evening
  at camp?"
- An NPC asks the PC a personal question.
- A moment of beauty or horror that invites an emotional
  reaction.
- A shared scene between two PCs that explores their
  relationship.

Design note: Schedule at least one character moment per
session, ideally after a high-tension scene. The contrast
creates pacing variety and gives players breathing room.

### Touchpoint Planning Matrix

Use this matrix during session prep to ensure coverage:

```
SESSION: _______________

| PC | Touchpoint Type | Description | When in Session |
|----|----------------|-------------|-----------------|
|    |                |             |                 |
|    |                |             |                 |
|    |                |             |                 |
|    |                |             |                 |

COVERAGE CHECK:
- [ ] Every PC has at least one touchpoint
- [ ] The B-plot PC has a high-impact touchpoint
- [ ] No PC has been without a high-impact touchpoint for 3+ sessions
- [ ] Touchpoint types vary across PCs (not all ability showcases)
```

## Connecting PC Arcs to the Main Plot

The strongest campaigns make personal arcs and the main plot
feel inseparable. When the villain is personally relevant to
each PC, when backstory elements appear as clues in the main
mystery, and when PC choices shape the campaign's direction,
the result is a campaign where everything matters.

### The Web of Connections

During campaign planning (not session-by-session), map how
each PC's backstory connects to the main arc.

```
MAIN ARC: _______________
MAIN ANTAGONIST: _______________

| PC | Backstory Element | Connection to Main Arc |
|----|-------------------|------------------------|
|    |                   |                        |
|    |                   |                        |
|    |                   |                        |
|    |                   |                        |
```

If a PC has no natural connection, create one:
- The antagonist's actions affected someone in the PC's past.
- The PC's organisation is a target or tool of the antagonist.
- The PC's homeland, family, or community is threatened.
- The PC possesses knowledge, an item, or a trait the
  antagonist needs or fears.

### PC Backstory as Clue Source

In investigation-heavy campaigns (especially Call of Cthulhu),
use PC backstories to generate clues:

- A PC's academic background means they recognise an obscure
  reference.
- A PC's criminal contacts can identify a suspect.
- A PC's family connection opens a door that would otherwise
  be closed.
- A PC's military experience lets them interpret evidence
  others would miss.

This simultaneously advances the investigation and validates
the player's character choices. The Three Clue Rule (see
gm-session-patterns.md) still applies — backstory-sourced
clues are one of the three paths, not the only path.

### Faction Membership as Personal Stakes

When PCs belong to factions:
- Faction goals should occasionally conflict with personal
  goals, creating tension.
- Faction NPCs should have relationships with the PC that
  evolve over time.
- Faction advancement should require choices that have
  personal costs.
- Faction politics should intersect with the main arc so
  the PC's faction membership is plot-relevant.

## System-Specific PC Arc Drivers

### Call of Cthulhu 7e

CoC investigators are fragile, mortal, and doomed to
encounter things beyond human comprehension. Their arcs
trend toward tragedy, but the best arcs have moments of
defiance, sacrifice, and hard-won understanding.

**Character Motivation Systems:**
Character motivation systems — like the Drive mechanic in
GUMSHOE games (Robin Laws / Pelgrane Press) — can anchor
session planning around what each PC is compelled to pursue.
Assign or identify each character's core motivation and build
session touchpoints that challenge or advance it.

**Arc fuel for CoC:**
- Sanity loss as arc progression. As SAN decreases, the
  investigator's grip on normal life weakens. Their arc
  is partly defined by what they sacrifice to pursue the
  truth.
- Personal connections under threat. Investigators who have
  family, friends, or dependents risk losing them to the
  Mythos or to their own growing obsession.
- Forbidden knowledge dilemmas. Each Mythos revelation
  costs SAN but provides capability. The arc question is:
  how far will you go?
- Backup investigator foreshadowing. Since investigators
  die, establishing the next investigator as an NPC
  in the current investigator's story creates narrative
  continuity.

**Per-session PC touchpoints in CoC:**
- A clue that connects to the investigator's occupation
  or background.
- A personal NPC who is affected by or drawn into the
  current scenario.
- A moment where the investigator's Drive compels them
  forward despite the danger.
- A sanity-testing event that reflects the investigator's
  specific fears or traumas.

### D&D 5e (2024 Revision)

D&D characters are heroes on an upward trajectory. Their arcs
trend toward growth, mastery, and the accumulation of power
and influence. The 2024 revision's background system provides
built-in arc material.

**Background-Driven Arcs:**
Each background provides personality traits, ideals, bonds,
and flaws. These four elements are the skeleton of a
character arc:
- The **ideal** defines what the character believes.
- The **bond** defines who or what the character cares about.
- The **flaw** defines the character's vulnerability.
- The arc tests the ideal, threatens the bond, and exploits
  the flaw.

**Arc fuel for D&D:**
- Personal quests tied to backstory (find the lost mentor,
  avenge the destroyed village, recover the family heirloom).
- Patron relationships that create obligations and rewards.
- Subclass identity moments where the PC's chosen path
  defines the narrative (a vengeance paladin must decide
  if revenge is still worth pursuing).
- Tier transitions (levels 5, 11, 17) as natural arc
  milestones where the character's relationship to the
  world fundamentally changes.

**Per-session PC touchpoints in D&D:**
- An encounter designed around one PC's class abilities or
  background features.
- An NPC connected to one PC's bond or backstory.
- A magic item or reward that advances one PC's personal
  quest.
- A moral choice that tests one PC's ideal or exploits
  their flaw.

### GURPS 4e

GURPS characters are defined by their advantages,
disadvantages, and skills. The point-buy system means every
character trait was a deliberate investment, and arcs should
honour those investments.

**Disadvantage-Driven Arcs:**
GURPS disadvantages are the most powerful arc drivers in the
system. Each disadvantage is a built-in story hook:
- **Duty** and **Sense of Duty** create obligations that
  generate adventure hooks and arc beats whenever the duty
  conflicts with personal desires.
- **Dependents** create NPCs who can be threatened,
  kidnapped, corrupted, or placed in danger. The frequency
  of appearance roll determines how often this arc driver
  activates.
- **Enemy** provides a recurring antagonist with a personal
  connection to the PC.
- **Obsession**, **Compulsive Behaviour**, and **Addiction**
  create internal conflicts that can escalate into crisis
  points.
- **Secret** provides a ticking time bomb — the arc
  question is whether the secret will be revealed and what
  happens when it is.
- **Vow** and **Code of Honour** create moral constraints
  that generate dilemmas.

**Arc fuel for GURPS:**
- The disadvantage activation pattern. Like a TV show,
  rotate which disadvantage drives the B-plot: one session
  focuses on the duty-bound soldier's orders, the next on
  the thief's enemy, the next on the scholar's obsession.
- Point-buy evolution. As characters earn points, they can
  buy off disadvantages — which is itself an arc beat
  (the character has overcome their flaw) or acquire new
  ones (the character has new problems).
- Skill-based showcase moments. GURPS has hundreds of
  skills. Design encounters that make each PC's unique
  skill investments the key to success.

**Per-session PC touchpoints in GURPS:**
- A disadvantage activation that forces a choice.
- An encounter requiring a PC's specific skill or advantage.
- A consequence from a previous disadvantage-driven decision.
- An opportunity to buy off a disadvantage or earn points
  toward character goals.

### Forged in the Dark

FitD characters are driven by vice, trauma, and the crew's
shared ambitions. The system has built-in arc mechanics that
the GM can leverage during session planning.

**Vice and Trauma as Arc Drivers:**
- **Vice** is the character's coping mechanism for stress.
  Indulging vice during downtime creates character-defining
  scenes. Overindulgence creates complications that ripple
  into future sessions.
- **Trauma** is acquired when stress overflows. Traumas
  (Cold, Haunted, Obsessed, Paranoid, Reckless, Soft,
  Unstable, Vicious) permanently alter the character's
  personality and create new narrative directions.
- Four traumas retire the character — creating a natural
  arc endpoint.

**Arc fuel for FitD:**
- Vice scenes during downtime. Each PC's vice indulgence
  is a built-in character moment. Design vice purveyors
  as recurring NPCs with their own agendas.
- Trauma escalation. As characters accumulate trauma, their
  behaviour shifts. A character who gains "Haunted" after
  encountering a ghost should see that ghost's influence
  in future sessions.
- Crew advancement as shared arc. The crew's reputation,
  territory, and upgrades create collective milestones
  that each PC experiences through their own lens.
- Entanglements as personal complications. After each score,
  the entanglement roll can generate problems that target
  specific PCs.
- Belief-equivalent: Long-term project clocks. PCs pursuing
  personal projects during downtime create self-directed
  arc progression.

**Per-session PC touchpoints in FitD:**
- A downtime scene centred on the PC's vice or personal
  project.
- An entanglement that targets the PC's relationships or
  territory.
- A score element designed to test the PC's specific action
  ratings.
- A faction move that threatens something the PC personally
  cares about.

## The Session Planning Workflow

Putting it all together, here is the complete workflow for
planning a session with per-PC content.

### Step 1: PC Roster Review

Complete the PC Roster Review Template (above) for every PC.
Time: 10-15 minutes.

### Step 2: Arc Check

Review the Arc Tracker. Identify which PC is due for their
next arc beat. Determine the A/B/C plot structure for this
session. Time: 5 minutes.

### Step 3: Touchpoint Assignment

Complete the Touchpoint Planning Matrix. Assign at least one
touchpoint per PC, with the B-plot PC getting a high-impact
touchpoint. Time: 10 minutes.

### Step 4: Scene Design

Design scenes that deliver the planned touchpoints. Where
possible, embed personal content in group scenes rather than
splitting the party. Use the scene templates from
content-generation.md.

**Open interaction windows:** Every social or event-based
scene must include an open interaction window before its
central event. See `scene-encounter-patterns.md` (Open
Interaction Windows) for the required elements: physical
space, available NPCs, observable details, timing guidance,
and transition trigger. Omitting the open window is a
common failure that removes player agency from structured
scenes. Time: 15-20 minutes.

### Step 5: Connection Audit

Verify that personal content connects to the main arc.
Check:
- Does the B-plot advance both the PC's personal arc and
  the campaign's main plot?
- Do touchpoints feel organic to the session's events, or
  forced?
- Are any PCs receiving only mechanical content (combat,
  skill checks) without narrative content?
Time: 5 minutes.

### Step 6: Spotlight Forecast

Estimate how spotlight time will distribute across the
planned session. If any PC is below 15% in a 4-player
party, redesign a scene to include them more actively.
Time: 5 minutes.

### Step 7: Player Agency Audit and Canon Grounding

This is the final verification step before a session plan is
considered complete. It catches the two most damaging
categories of error in AI-assisted session planning: player
agency violations and hallucinated facts.

**Player Agency Audit:**
Review every scene in the plan against these rules:

- No sentence describes a PC's emotions or internal state.
- No sentence assumes a PC takes a specific action.
- All PC-facing content uses conditional language ("if the
  PC does X" rather than "the PC does X").
- Every NPC confrontation scene has at least two response
  contingencies (cooperative and hostile). Never assume
  players won't initiate combat, even in social settings.
- Read-aloud text describes only objective sensory
  information (sight, sound, smell, touch) in 2-4 sentences
  maximum. No emotions, no perceptions, no thoughts.
- Touchpoint scenes describe what is AVAILABLE to the PC,
  not what the PC DOES with it.

Reference: scenario-writing.md, "Player Agency Constraints"
section, for the full anti-pattern table and conditional
language examples.

**Canon Grounding Check:**
Review every factual claim in the plan:

- Every NPC background detail, location, relationship, and
  resource must trace to an existing source file.
- Every contact or option offered to the PCs must be
  established through prior play (contacts not yet met
  cannot offer services; locations not yet discovered
  cannot be refuges).
- Any unverified claim should be flagged for Keeper
  confirmation rather than stated as established canon.

Reference: continuity-engine.md, "Canon Grounding Check"
procedure, for the full detection steps and common
hallucination patterns.

Time: 10-15 minutes. This step is non-negotiable. Skipping
it risks producing content the Keeper must mentally rewrite
at the table.

Total session prep time for PC-focused planning: 60-75
minutes. This is on top of standard prep (encounters,
maps, NPC stat blocks) but can replace some of it —
character-focused prep naturally generates encounters,
NPCs, and scenes.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**The Protagonist Problem.** One PC's arc dominates the
campaign while others serve as supporting cast. Rotation
and tracking prevent this.

**The Backstory Dump.** Revealing too much of a PC's
backstory in a single session. Dole out backstory
connections across many sessions for sustained engagement.

**The Forced Connection.** Making every campaign element
somehow tied to every PC's past. Some elements should be
new to everyone. The world is bigger than the PCs' histories.

**The Solo Spotlight.** Giving a PC their personal scene
while other players sit idle. Embed personal content in
group scenes instead.

**The Neglected Quiet Player.** Assuming a quiet player
does not want spotlight time. Offer the opportunity; let
them decline. Provide written notes or one-on-one scenes
between sessions if they prefer.

**The Arc Railroad.** Predetermining how a PC's arc will
resolve. The GM creates the situations; the player decides
the outcome. If the "crisis" has only one acceptable
resolution, it is not a crisis — it is a cutscene.

**The Stale Arc.** Failing to advance a PC's arc for 4+
sessions. Even a small C-plot clue keeps the arc alive in
the player's mind.
