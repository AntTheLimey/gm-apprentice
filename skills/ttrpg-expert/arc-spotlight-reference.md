# Character Arcs, Spotlight, and Touchpoints

GM frameworks for managing character arcs, balancing spotlight
across the party, and designing per-PC touchpoints. This is
advisory reference material — theory, thresholds, and patterns
rather than step-by-step procedures.

For **session prep workflows** that apply these frameworks, see
`session-prep` (the skill that owns the planning workflow).
For **system-specific arc drivers** (how CoC SAN loss, GURPS
disadvantages, FitD trauma, and D&D backgrounds fuel arcs),
see the per-system `session-procedures.md` files.

## Five-Stage Arc Model

Character arcs follow a TV-series structure: personal
storylines weaving through the ensemble narrative. Each arc
passes through five stages, though duration and pacing vary
by campaign and character.

| Stage | Duration | What Happens | GM Actions |
|-------|----------|-------------|------------|
| 1. Establishment | Sessions 1-3 | PC's beliefs, flaws, and goals are introduced | Record stated values. Introduce NPCs who reflect or challenge PC identity. Plant backstory seeds in the world. |
| 2. Testing | Sessions 3-10+ (longest stage) | Situations test beliefs, strain relationships, and complicate goals | Design encounters probing stated values. Present moral dilemmas with no clean answer. Introduce NPCs with opposing but sympathetic views. Offer temptations away from stated goals. |
| 3. Crisis | 1-2 sessions (after 2-3 sessions of escalation) | A turning point — defining choice, confronting a flaw, facing consequences | Build pressure over 2-3 sessions before the crisis. Stakes should be personal and irreversible. The party witnesses or participates. Never predetermine the outcome. |
| 4. Transformation | 1-3 sessions | The character changes as a result of the crisis — new belief, hollow victory, loss, or growth | Reflect change through NPC reactions and world consequences. Let the player update beliefs and goals. Introduce new complications arising from the transformation. |
| 5. New Equilibrium | 1-2 sessions | The changed state settles and becomes the new Establishment for the next arc | Acknowledge the completed arc. Plant seeds for the next one. Use the completed arc as connective tissue linking to other PCs. |

Every arc has a **theme** — a short phrase capturing the core
tension (e.g. "loyalty vs. freedom", "confronting the past").
The theme guides which situations to create and which NPCs to
involve.

**Stagger arcs across the party.** Advancing every PC's arc
simultaneously dilutes each one and overwhelms the session.
Move one or two arcs forward per session, letting others rest.
A PC in Crisis (Stage 3) naturally demands more attention and
may need consecutive sessions of focus.

## A/B/C Plot Structure

Sessions balance three layers of content, each serving a
different narrative purpose.

| Plot | Share | Description |
|------|-------|------------|
| A-Plot | 50-60% | Main campaign storyline involving all PCs |
| B-Plot | 25-35% | Personal arc for one "featured" PC this session |
| C-Plot | 10-15% | Lighter personal touch for a second PC — a brief scene, callback, or arc clue |

The **B-plot rotation** ensures every PC gets featured within
N sessions, where N equals party size. A PC in mid-crisis
(Stage 3) may warrant consecutive B-plots. A PC who received
C-plot treatment in session N is a natural B-plot candidate
for session N+1 or N+2.

## Spotlight Theory

Spotlight balance is not about equal screen time in every
session — it is about equitable engagement over a span of
sessions. The key thresholds:

- **15% floor:** In a four-player party, any PC falling below
  roughly 15% of a session's spotlight signals a problem
  requiring corrective planning in the next session.
- **Sustained imbalance (3+ sessions):** When a PC has been
  underserved for three or more consecutive sessions, the
  imbalance is serious and demands immediate corrective action
  — a B-plot feature, a high-impact touchpoint, or a scenario
  element built around that PC's strengths.

Corrective approaches include designing the next session's
B-plot around the neglected PC, creating an encounter that
showcases their unique abilities, or introducing a backstory
element that puts them at the centre of a scene.

## Weaving Personal Content into Group Scenes

Splitting the party for dedicated personal scenes leaves other
players idle. Stronger results come from embedding personal
content into group encounters using four techniques:

- **Embed in group encounters:** The party visits a military
  outpost and the former soldier PC's personal content emerges
  naturally from the location.
- **NPCs as vectors:** A backstory NPC appears in a scene
  already involving the whole party, making the personal
  connection part of the shared experience.
- **Multi-hook encounters:** A single scene contains elements
  appealing to different PCs — an informant for the rogue, a
  debtor for the paladin, a rare book for the wizard, a hidden
  shrine for the cleric.
- **Witness and react:** Personal moments gain dramatic weight
  when other PCs are present to respond, support, or
  complicate. The audience matters.

## Touchpoint Taxonomy

Every session should include at least one touchpoint per PC.
Not all touchpoints need to be major — some simply signal that
the campaign remembers the character exists. The six types,
ranked by narrative impact:

| Type | Impact | Description | Design Notes |
|------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Backstory Connection | High | NPC, location, or event from PC's history appears | Use sparingly: 1-2 per arc. Overuse makes the world feel artificially centred on the PCs. |
| Moral Dilemma | High | Forces a choice between competing values, targeting specific PC beliefs | No clean answer. If the "right" choice is obvious, it is not a dilemma. |
| Ability Showcase | Medium | Encounter designed so one PC's specific skills are key to success | Rotate across PCs. Avoid repeatedly showcasing the same character. |
| Decision Callback | Medium | World reacts to a previous PC choice, delayed 2-5 sessions | Immediate callbacks feel reactive; delayed ones feel like world memory. Track decisions in post-session notes. |
| Arc Advancement Clue | Medium | A clue or hint advancing the PC's personal arc | Ideal C-plot touchpoint. Brief, suggestive, forward-looking. |
| Character Moment | Low-Med | Quiet scene for self-expression without plot pressure (campfire, downtime) | Schedule at least one per session after high-tension scenes for pacing variety. |

**Frequency guidance:** B-plot PCs should receive at least one
high-impact touchpoint per session. No PC should go three or
more sessions without a high-impact touchpoint. Touchpoint
types should vary across PCs within a session — avoid giving
everyone the same kind.

## Connecting PC Arcs to Main Plot

**Web of connections:** During campaign planning (not
session-by-session), map how each PC's backstory connects to
the main arc. Every PC should have at least one organic link.
When no natural connection exists, consider: the antagonist
affected the PC's past, the PC's organisation is a target, the
PC's community is threatened, or the PC possesses something
the antagonist needs or fears.

**Backstory as clue source:** Particularly valuable in
investigation campaigns. A PC's background lets them recognise
references, identify suspects, open doors, or interpret
evidence others would miss. Backstory clues should be one of
at least three paths to the information — never the only path.

**Faction membership:** Factions deepen arcs when their goals
occasionally conflict with the PC's personal goals, when
faction NPCs evolve over time, when advancement demands costly
choices, and when faction politics intersect the main arc.
Static factions that only serve as allies or resources are a
missed opportunity.

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Prevention |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Protagonist Problem | One PC's arc dominates the campaign | B-plot rotation and spotlight tracking |
| Backstory Dump | Too much backstory revealed in one session | Dole out backstory across many sessions |
| Forced Connection | Every element tied to every PC's past | Some elements should be new to everyone |
| Solo Spotlight | Personal scene while other players sit idle | Embed personal content in group scenes |
| Neglected Quiet Player | Assuming quiet means the player does not want spotlight | Offer opportunity; provide written notes between sessions |
| Arc Railroad | Predetermining how the arc resolves | GM creates situations; the player decides the outcome |
| Stale Arc | No arc advancement for 4+ sessions | Even a small C-plot clue keeps an arc alive |
