# Random and Procedural Generation

Frameworks for procedural content generation including oracle
systems, random tables, name generators, and emergent content
creation. This document covers both structured random generation
(tables with curated entries) and procedural generation (rules
that produce novel combinations).

## When to Use Random Generation

Random generation serves the GM in specific situations:

- **Improvisation support.** The PCs go somewhere unplanned and
  the GM needs content immediately.
- **Inspiration.** The GM has creative block and needs a spark.
- **Surprise.** The GM wants to be surprised by their own
  campaign, generating content they would not have planned.
- **Volume.** The campaign needs many similar elements (a list
  of taverns, a roster of guards, a catalogue of rumours) and
  hand-crafting each one is impractical.
- **Fairness.** Random encounters and events feel impartial
  when generated by dice rather than GM fiat.

Random generation should not replace intentional design for
narratively critical content. The climactic villain, the
central mystery, and the key emotional beats of a campaign
deserve deliberate crafting.

## Oracle Systems

### The Yes/No Oracle

The simplest oracle answers yes/no questions about the fiction.
When the GM asks "Is the door locked?" or "Does the NPC
recognise the PCs?", the oracle provides an answer.

**Basic Oracle (d6):**

| Roll | Result |
|------|--------|
| 1 | No, and... (worse than expected) |
| 2 | No |
| 3 | No, but... (silver lining) |
| 4 | Yes, but... (complication) |
| 5 | Yes |
| 6 | Yes, and... (better than expected) |

**Probability-Modified Oracle:**

Adjust the threshold based on likelihood:

| Likelihood | Yes on... |
|-----------|-----------|
| Very unlikely | 6 only |
| Unlikely | 5-6 |
| Even odds | 4-6 |
| Likely | 3-6 |
| Very likely | 2-6 |
| Nearly certain | Automatic yes, roll for "and..." |

### Name Generation by Setting

Maintain pre-generated name lists organised by setting and
culture. Names should be:

- Pronounceable at the table (avoid names players stumble over).
- Distinct from each other (different starting letters and
  syllable counts).
- Setting-appropriate (period and culture).
- Memorable (one or two syllables for minor NPCs, longer for
  major ones).

**Name bank management:**
- Keep 20-30 unused names ready per setting.
- Cross off names as they are assigned.
- Record the assignment immediately to prevent reuse.
- Group names by apparent gender and social class if the setting
  differentiates these.

## Random Encounter Generation

### Encounter Table Structure

Effective encounter tables combine multiple elements:

**Layer 1 — Who/What (d12 or d20):**
The creature, NPC type, or environmental hazard.

**Layer 2 — What They Are Doing (d8):**

| Roll | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 1 | Fleeing from something |
| 2 | Searching for something |
| 3 | Guarding a location or item |
| 4 | Engaged in a ritual or task |
| 5 | Fighting each other |
| 6 | Setting an ambush |
| 7 | Resting or injured |
| 8 | Travelling with a purpose |

**Layer 3 — Complication (d6):**

| Roll | Complication |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Weather or environmental change |
| 2 | A third party arrives |
| 3 | The terrain is hazardous |
| 4 | One side has information the PCs need |
| 5 | A moral dilemma is present |
| 6 | The encounter is not what it appears |

### Encounter Table Principles

- Weight entries by frequency. Common encounters appear on
  multiple numbers; rare encounters on single numbers.
- Include non-combat entries. At least 30% of encounters should
  be social, environmental, or informational.
- Connect encounters to the current arc. The random table for
  a region where cultists are active should include cultist-
  related encounters.
- Re-roll or discard results that break continuity or tone.
  Random does not mean mandatory.

| Roll | Hook Pattern |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Someone important goes missing |

| 3 | A strange phenomenon occurs |
| 4 | An old enemy resurfaces |
| 5 | A plea for help from a stranger |
| 6 | A discovery in an unexpected place |
| 7 | A deadline creates urgency |
| 8 | A secret is accidentally revealed |
| 9 | Two factions come into open conflict |
| 10 | An NPC ally is in danger |
| 11 | A prophecy or warning is delivered |
| 12 | Travel is disrupted (road blocked, ship delayed) |
| 13 | A festival or event provides cover for villainy |
| 14 | An inheritance or legacy creates obligations |
| 15 | A natural disaster threatens the area |
| 16 | A trusted institution is revealed as corrupt |
| 17 | A new arrival disrupts local power dynamics |
| 18 | Something that was dead or dormant awakens |
| 19 | A moral dilemma with no clean solution |
| 20 | The consequences of a past PC action arrive |

### Using Hooks

- Roll 2-3 hooks and combine them for complexity.
- Connect the hook to existing campaign threads when possible.
- Present hooks through NPCs, events, or environmental change
  rather than exposition.
- Always provide enough information for the PCs to make an
  informed decision about whether to engage.

## Rumour Tables

### Rumour Template

Each rumour has three components:

- **Content:** What the rumour claims.
- **Truth Value:** True, partially true, or false.
- **Source:** Who is spreading this and why.

**Example Rumour Table (d8):**

| Roll | Rumour | Truth | Source |
|------|--------|-------|--------|
| 1 | "The old mill is haunted." | Partially true: someone is hiding there | Local children who explored |
| 2 | "The mayor owes money to the thieves' guild." | True | Disgruntled guild member |
| 3 | "Travellers have been disappearing on the north road." | True | Merchant who lost a caravan |
| 4 | "The new priest is actually a wanted criminal." | False, but the priest has secrets | Jealous rival |

### Rumour Design Principles

- True rumours should point toward active plot threads.
- Partially true rumours should contain enough truth to be
  useful and enough falsehood to be misleading.
- False rumours should reflect the fears or prejudices of the
  source rather than being arbitrary lies.
- Avoid creating deliberate red herrings. Players generate
  their own false leads from partial information.

## Procedural Generation Principles

### Combination Over Invention

The most effective procedural generation combines existing
elements in new ways rather than inventing from nothing. A
"fire-breathing spider" is more evocative than an original
creature because it combines two known concepts.

### Constraint-Driven Generation

When generating content, constraints produce better results
than open prompts:

- "Generate an NPC" produces generic results.
- "Generate a CoC 7e NPC who works at the university, opposes
  the cult, and has a secret connection to the victim"
  produces a character embedded in the campaign.

### Serendipity as a Feature

Random generation sometimes produces surprising combinations
that the GM would never have planned. These surprises are a
feature, not a bug. The GM's job is to interpret the random
result in the most interesting way possible, not to re-roll
until they get something predictable.

### When to Override

Random results should be overridden when they:

- Contradict established canon.
- Break the campaign's tone or genre.
- Create an unsafe situation at the table.
- Are simply not fun or interesting for this group.

The GM always has final authority over random results.
