# Scenario and Encounter Writing

Guidance for writing RPG scenario and encounter text that
respects player agency and serves the GM as reference.
Scenarios are instruction manuals for GMs, not stories for
readers.

## Core Principle

The writer tells the GM everything up front. Villain's plan,
NPC motivations, clue connections, timeline, possible endings
— all appear early. Never withhold information from the GM to
create surprise; the GM needs complete knowledge to run
effectively.

## Narrative Voice

### Read-Aloud Text (Boxed Text)

- Objective sensory descriptions only. "The bloated corpse
  dominates the clearing" — not "You see a corpse and feel
  uneasy."
- 2-4 sentences maximum
- Only immediately perceivable (sight, sound, smell, touch)
- No game-mechanical language ("stunned", "frightened")
- No hedging ("seems", "appears", "looks like" — signals
  metagaming)
- Read aloud during writing to catch awkward phrasing

**Alternative:** italicised sensory bullet points the GM
paraphrases. More flexible at the table.

### GM-Only Text

Scannable during play: bold headers, bullet points, short
paragraphs. GMs referencing mid-session can't parse dense prose.

## Player Agency Constraints

### Hooks, Not Rails

≥3 hooks pointing toward core engagement (same logic as Three
Clue Rule): personal, professional, curiosity, proximity,
moral, material, or threat-based. PCs decide whether to bite.

### Conditional Language

"If the investigators visit the library..." — not "The
investigators visit the library..."

### Anti-Patterns (Railroading)

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Assumed actions | "You rush to help" | "NPC calls for help" |
| Single-solution | Only one answer works | Multiple valid approaches |
| Floating locations | Any inn has the quest | Real choices with outcomes |
| Illusory choices | All roads lead to same | Distinct paths with content |
| Plot-gated content | Nothing happens off-plot | World advances on its own |
| Forced monologues | Villain explains anyway | PCs must earn information |

### Situation-Based Design

The Alexandrian: prepare situations (locations, NPCs,
motivations, resources, threats), not sequences. The scenario
defines what exists; PCs determine what happens.

## NPC Approach Patterns

### Proactive NPCs

Five components per significant NPC: Goal, Plan, Timeline,
Reaction, Escalation. When PCs ignore an NPC, their plan
advances along the timeline; consequences eventually reach
the PCs.

### Proactive Clues (when players stall)

- Someone attempts to kill a PC
- Villain's plan advances visibly
- NPC contacts PCs with warning
- Environmental changes signal worsening

### Reluctant PCs

Tie hooks to backstories; make problem local; offer multiple
entry points; let NPCs seek PCs; advance timeline; reward
engagement visibly.

## Encounter Text Structure

| Section | Content | Audience |
|---|---|---|
| Title | Name + optional threat level | GM |
| Read-Aloud | Sensory description | Players via GM |
| Background | Why this encounter exists | GM |
| NPC Motivations | Wants, fears, plans | GM |
| Creatures/Hazards | Stats, tactics, behaviour | GM |
| Skill Checks | Relevant checks + targets | GM |
| Clues/Information | What PCs can discover | GM |
| Outcomes | Success, failure, flight | GM |
| Treasure/Rewards | Material + narrative | GM |
| Connections | Where this leads next | GM |

### Writing Principles

- Front-load essentials: what's happening, who's present,
  what they want
- Separate immediate facts from contingent facts
- NPC behaviour rather than dialogue scripts
- Include failure states for every check/combat

### Three-Stage Encounters

1. **Setup** — situation, initial conditions
2. **Complication** — mid-encounter change (reinforcements,
   environment, revelations, betrayals)
3. **Payoff** — maximum stakes, resolution

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming PC actions:** "The PCs enter the cave" → "If the
  PCs enter the cave." "You feel dread" → "The air is cold
  and damp."
- **Linear plots:** single missed clue halts progress. Use
  node-based design instead.
- **Gating behind single check:** use Three Clue Rule,
  GUMSHOE automatic clues, or fail-forward.
- **Excessive backstory:** brief, placed where GM needs context.
- **Deliberate red herrings:** players generate their own.
- **Hiding GM info:** GM discovers facts at player pace →
  undermines ability to run. GM must know everything from start.
- **Scripted dialogue:** constrains GM, feels stilted. Provide
  personality + motivation + key info instead.

## System-Specific Conventions

### CoC 7e

Keeper / Investigator / Scenario. Petersen's Onion Layer:
surface looks conventional, each layer more disturbing (Hint →
Clear danger → The monster). Handouts central, numbered.
Investigation > combat. Multiple clue paths. Address "PCs call
the police." Percentile skill stat blocks.

### D&D 5e (2024)

DM / Character / Adventure. Four-step design: Premise → Draw
in → Plan encounters → End. Three difficulty levels (Low/
Moderate/High). Numbered location-based structure. Boxed text
conventions.

### GURPS 4e

Detailed NPC characterization via disadvantages/quirks/
motivations. Multi-sensory descriptions. Specify relevant
skills/mechanics per scene. Include debugging for PC deviations.

### Forged in the Dark

Score / Obstacle / Scoundrel. Box/House/Target framework.
Plan obstacles not solutions. Engagement roll skips detailed
planning. Flashbacks eliminate need to anticipate PC plans.
Progress clocks for sustained effort. Menu of obstacles per
zone, not fixed sequence.
