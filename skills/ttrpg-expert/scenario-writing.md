# Scenario and Encounter Writing

Guidance for writing RPG scenario and encounter text that
respects player agency and serves the GM as reference.
Scenarios are instruction manuals for GMs, not stories for
readers.

**System-specific scenario conventions:** for system mechanics
and stat blocks when writing scenarios, also read:
- CoC 7e: `systems/coc-7e/session-procedures.md`, `systems/coc-7e/creatures.md`
- D&D 5e: `systems/dnd-5e-2024/monsters.md`, `systems/dnd-5e-2024/conditions-rules.md`
- PF2e: `systems/pf2e/monsters.md`, `systems/pf2e/session-procedures.md`
- GURPS 4e: `systems/gurps-4e/combat.md`
- FitD: `systems/fitd/rules-reference.md`

## Core Principle

The writer tells the GM everything up front. Villain's plan,
NPC motivations, clue connections, timeline, possible endings
— all appear early. Never withhold information from the GM to
create surprise; the GM needs complete knowledge to run
effectively.

## Narrative Voice

### Read-Aloud Text (Boxed Text)

- Objective sensory descriptions only. "The bloated corpse
  dominates the clearing" — not "You see a corpse and feel
  uneasy."
- 2-4 sentences maximum
- Only immediately perceivable (sight, sound, smell, touch)
- No game-mechanical language ("stunned", "frightened")
- No hedging ("seems", "appears", "looks like" — signals
  metagaming)
- Read aloud during writing to catch awkward phrasing

**Alternative:** italicised sensory bullet points the GM
paraphrases. More flexible at the table.

### GM-Only Text

Scannable during play: bold headers, bullet points, short
paragraphs. GMs referencing mid-session can't parse dense prose.

## Player Agency Constraints

These constraints are engineering, not etiquette. A scene engine
built on a **predicted PC response** is fragile: the moment the
player chooses otherwise, the hook collapses. A hook resting on
an NPC's want survives any player choice. (A repair that assumed
a PC would refuse food, was "visibly unwell", had "pushed away
two meals" — all invented, all the player's decisions — broke the
instant the player chose to eat. Rebuilt on the cook's want (she
needs a job done, puts a tray in the PC's arms), it runs no
matter what any PC does.) That is why the rule pays for itself,
independent of agency ethics.

### Hooks, Not Rails

≥3 hooks pointing toward core engagement (same logic as Three
Clue Rule): personal, professional, curiosity, proximity,
moral, material, or threat-based. PCs decide whether to bite.

### Conditional Language

"If the investigators visit the library..." — not "The
investigators visit the library..."

### Anti-Patterns (Railroading)

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Assumed actions | "You rush to help" | "NPC calls for help" |
| Single-solution | Only one answer works | Multiple valid approaches |
| Floating locations | Any inn has the quest | Real choices with outcomes |
| Illusory choices | All roads lead to same | Distinct paths with content |
| Plot-gated content | Nothing happens off-plot | World advances on its own |
| Forced monologues | Villain explains anyway | PCs must earn information |

### Situation-Based Design

The Alexandrian: prepare situations (locations, NPCs,
motivations, resources, threats), not sequences. The scenario
defines what exists; PCs determine what happens.

### Why Would This PC Go There?

The single highest-value question for any character scene:
**why would this PC go there, and who is making them?** A scene
that waits for a PC to arrive unprompted has no engine — it
describes a condition, and a condition has no way of starting.

A hook is an NPC who wants something from a specific PC and comes
and takes it; it does not wait to be discovered. Bruyère has not
seen France since 1801 and a viscount who was in Vienna three
weeks ago just walked into his inn — so Bruyère pours. Nafisa
wants a job done and puts a tray in the PC's arms. Each brings
the scene to the player in the first sixty seconds.

Do not confuse characterisation with a hook: an NPC being
reticent, unimpressible, or hard to charm describes what happens
once the PC is already in the room. A "hook" made of an NPC
conspicuously not engaging is worse than no hook.

### Adventure Shapes

Choose the structural skeleton before building content. The
shape constrains what content to develop and how PCs navigate.

| Shape | Description | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Linear | A → B → C. Fixed sequence. | One-shots, tutorials, tightly paced horror |
| Branching | Forks with distinct paths. Paths may reconverge. | Mystery with multiple suspect lines, moral dilemmas |
| Hub-and-spoke | Central base, radiating locations/missions. Return between. | Sandbox investigation, heist prep, faction play |
| Open-node | Interconnected nodes, any order. Each node self-contained. | Hexcrawl, pointcrawl, exploration campaigns |
| Sandbox | No predetermined structure. World reacts to PC actions. | Living-world campaigns, emergent play, FitD |

Linear is not inherently bad — a tightly paced one-shot benefits
from it. But flag when a concept needs branching and the GM is
building linear.

## NPC Approach Patterns

### Proactive NPCs

Five components per significant NPC: Goal, Plan, Timeline,
Reaction, Escalation. When PCs ignore an NPC, their plan
advances along the timeline; consequences eventually reach
the PCs.

**Victory-state design:** Start from what the world looks like
if the villain succeeds. Work backwards through 3-5 logistical
steps to reach that state. Each step becomes a pressure point
PCs can discover, disrupt, or accelerate. This prevents villain-
goal drift and naturally generates hooks at each stage.

### Proactive Clues (when players stall)

- Someone attempts to kill a PC
- Villain's plan advances visibly
- NPC contacts PCs with warning
- Environmental changes signal worsening

### Reluctant PCs

Tie hooks to backstories; make problem local; offer multiple
entry points; let NPCs seek PCs; advance timeline; reward
engagement visibly.

## Encounter Text Structure

| Section | Content | Audience |
|---|---|---|
| Title | Name + optional threat level | GM |
| Read-Aloud | Sensory description | Players via GM |
| Background | Why this encounter exists | GM |
| NPC Motivations | Wants, fears, plans | GM |
| Creatures/Hazards | Stats, tactics, behaviour | GM |
| Skill Checks | Relevant checks + targets | GM |
| Clues/Information | What PCs can discover | GM |
| Outcomes | Success, failure, flight | GM |
| Treasure/Rewards | Material + narrative | GM |
| Connections | Where this leads next | GM |

### Writing Principles

- Front-load essentials: what's happening, who's present,
  what they want
- Separate immediate facts from contingent facts
- NPC behaviour rather than dialogue scripts
- Include failure states for every check/combat
- A scene runs as long as its situation, initiator, branches, NPC
  wants, and mechanical notes require — earn the length, don't pad
  it. Cut bloat (restated theses, repetition, self-documentation,
  unusable purple prose), not substance. Over ~1,200 words,
  sanity-check that the length is load-bearing rather than bloat;
  it is a nudge, not a cap.

### Three-Stage Encounters

1. **Setup** — situation, initial conditions
2. **Complication** — mid-encounter change (reinforcements,
   environment, revelations, betrayals)
3. **Payoff** — maximum stakes, resolution

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming PC actions:** "The PCs enter the cave" → "If the
  PCs enter the cave." "You feel dread" → "The air is cold
  and damp."
- **Linear plots:** single missed clue halts progress. Use
  node-based design instead.
- **Gating behind single check:** use Three Clue Rule,
  GUMSHOE automatic clues, or fail-forward.
- **Excessive backstory:** brief, placed where GM needs context.
- **Deliberate red herrings:** players generate their own.
- **Hiding GM info:** GM discovers facts at player pace →
  undermines ability to run. GM must know everything from start.
- **Scripted dialogue:** constrains GM, feels stilted. Provide
  personality + motivation + key info instead.

### One-Shot Structural Constraints

One-shots need hard constraints at conception — there is no
next session to recover from loose threads:

- Single inciting event that cannot be ignored
- Contained location or social space (one building, one
  neighbourhood, one ship)
- Pre-generated or pre-tied PCs (no time for organic bonds)
- Resolution that closes within session (3-4 hours)
- No more than 3 key NPCs, 2 locations, 1 faction

### Few-Shot Guidance (2-8 Sessions)

Few-shots need campaign depth with one-shot closure. No
established framework exists — build from these principles:

- Single arc with a built-in ending (the ritual date, the
  ship's departure, the election)
- Session count should be explicit at conception ("this is
  a 4-session arc")
- Escalation tied to the timeline, not PC actions — the
  clock ticks whether they engage or not
- Final session must be satisfying even if PCs haven't
  resolved everything — the world moves on

### Playability Stress Test

Before committing to a concept, test it for RPG viability:

- Can the premise branch? If there's only one path through,
  it's a story, not an adventure.
- Does it have player agency? If the PCs are audience to
  events rather than participants, restructure.
- Does adapting from fiction require removing the fixed
  narrative? If yes, extract the setting and discard the
  plot.
- Can a session end mid-adventure without losing momentum?
  If not, it might need to be a one-shot.

## System-Specific Conventions

### CoC 7e

Keeper / Investigator / Scenario. Petersen's Onion Layer:
surface looks conventional, each layer more disturbing (Hint →
Clear danger → The monster). Handouts central, numbered.
Investigation > combat. Multiple clue paths. Address "PCs call
the police." Percentile skill stat blocks.

### D&D 5e (2024)

DM / Character / Adventure. Four-step design: Premise → Draw
in → Plan encounters → End. Three difficulty levels (Low/
Moderate/High). Numbered location-based structure. Boxed text
conventions.

### GURPS 4e

Detailed NPC characterization via disadvantages/quirks/
motivations. Multi-sensory descriptions. Specify relevant
skills/mechanics per scene. Include debugging for PC deviations.

### Forged in the Dark

Score / Obstacle / Scoundrel. Box/House/Target framework.
Plan obstacles not solutions. Engagement roll skips detailed
planning. Flashbacks eliminate need to anticipate PC plans.
Progress clocks for sustained effort. Menu of obstacles per
zone, not fixed sequence.
