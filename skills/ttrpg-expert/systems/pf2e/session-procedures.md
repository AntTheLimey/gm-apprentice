# Pathfinder 2e (Remaster) — Session Procedures

How to run a Pathfinder 2e session: modes of play, initiative, DCs, encounter building, treasure, rest, and session flow. This is the GM math home.

## Modes of Play

Pathfinder switches between three modes as the fiction demands. Announce the mode change; players adjust how they think about time and actions.

| Mode | When | Time scale | Stakes |
|------|------|-----------|--------|
| Encounter | Combat, a duel, a tense negotiation — any moment where each action could swing the outcome | 6-second rounds, strict initiative order | Moderate to high |
| Exploration | Movement between encounters: travel, searching, investigating, canvassing | ~10-minute increments, no rounds | Low to moderate |
| Downtime | Safe stretches of days or weeks: crafting, earning income, research, recovery | Days or longer, abstracted | Low |

- **Switching in:** call for initiative the moment someone commits to an action foes can't ignore (attack, drawn weapon, opening a debate). Drop back to exploration when the immediate threat resolves.
- **Switching out:** don't roll initiative just to end combat instantly — if everyone only wants to talk, stay in exploration or downtime.

## Encounter Mode

### Initiative

- Default check is **Perception**. Highest result acts first, down to lowest.
- **Alternative skills** are allowed when the fiction supports them, ideally set up beforehand: Stealth for a character Avoiding Notice, Deception for a treacherous surprise attack, Society for spotting a spy, or a magical skill for a caster studying a phenomenon. Don't let a player invent a reason on the spot just to use a favoured skill.
- **Hidden enemies:** anyone Avoiding Notice rolls Stealth for initiative; compare it to each foe's Perception DC to determine who is undetected.
- **Batch initiative:** roll once for a group of identical foes (e.g. four goblins) to keep the pace up; they still take individual turns.

### Round Structure

- Each round is 6 seconds. On their turn a creature has **3 actions** plus **1 reaction**, spent in any order.
- Most activities cost 1 action; some cost 2 or 3 (shown by action icons). Reactions trigger off specific events and refresh at the start of the creature's turn.
- The **multiple attack penalty** applies within a turn: −5 on a second attack, −10 on a third (−4/−8 with an agile weapon).
- Resolve turns in initiative order; play NPCs and hazards yourself when their slot comes up.

## Setting DCs

Two ways to pick a DC: a **simple DC** keyed to proficiency rank when the task has no level, or a **level-based DC** when the subject (creature, spell, item, obstacle) has a level.

### Simple DCs by Proficiency

| Proficiency Rank | DC |
|------------------|-----|
| Untrained | 10 |
| Trained | 15 |
| Expert | 20 |
| Master | 30 |
| Legendary | 40 |

### DCs by Level

|-------|-----|-------|-----|-------|-----|
| 0 | 14 | 9 | 26 | 18 | 38 |
| 1 | 15 | 10 | 27 | 19 | 39 |
| 2 | 16 | 11 | 28 | 20 | 40 |
| 3 | 18 | 12 | 30 | 21 | 42 |
| 4 | 19 | 13 | 31 | 22 | 44 |
| 5 | 20 | 14 | 32 | 23 | 46 |
| 6 | 22 | 15 | 34 | 24 | 48 |
| 7 | 23 | 16 | 35 | 25 | 50 |
| 8 | 24 | 17 | 36 | | |

For spells, use the spell rank on the 1–10 scale: rank 1 = DC 15, 2 = 18, 3 = 20, 4 = 23, 5 = 26, 6 = 28, 7 = 31, 8 = 34, 9 = 36, 10 = 39.

### DC Adjustments

Apply an adjustment when a task is unusually easy or hard for its level, or when the subject is uncommon, rare, or unique. Rarity and difficulty share the same steps.

| Difficulty | Adjustment | Matching Rarity |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Incredibly easy | −10 | — |
| Very easy | −5 | — |
| Easy | −2 | — |
| Hard | +2 | Uncommon |
| Very hard | +5 | Rare |
| Incredibly hard | +10 | Unique |

Names are relative to the task's own level: a "hard" 2nd-level task is not hard for a 10th-level PC. At high levels many groups find the "very hard" step reads as merely standard — bump up when you need a genuine challenge.

## Encounter Building

### XP Budget by Threat

Pick a threat level, then spend its XP budget on creatures and hazards. The budget assumes a party of four. Per extra PC, add the Character Adjustment; per missing PC, subtract it. XP *awards* never change — always give the four-PC value.

| Threat | XP Budget | Per Extra/Missing PC |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|
| Trivial | 40 or less | 10 |
| Low | 60 | 20 |
| Moderate | 80 | 20 |
| Severe | 120 | 30 |
| Extreme | 160 | 40 |

- **Trivial / Low:** warm-ups; little chance the party is endangered.
- **Moderate:** a real test requiring sound tactics and resource management.
- **Severe:** the hardest most groups can reliably beat; fitting for a boss, with a genuine chance a PC dies.
- **Extreme:** an even match — use only when you accept a possible TPK (campaign climax, veteran table).

### Creature XP by Level Relative to Party

Each creature costs XP from the budget based on its level minus party level.

| Creature Level | XP | Suggested Role |
|----------------|-----|----------------|
| Party − 4 | 10 | Low-threat lackey |
| Party − 3 | 15 | Low/moderate lackey |
| Party − 2 | 20 | Any lackey or standard |
| Party − 1 | 30 | Any standard creature |
| Party level | 40 | Standard, or low-threat boss |
| Party + 1 | 60 | Low/moderate boss |
| Party + 2 | 80 | Moderate/severe boss |
| Party + 3 | 120 | Severe/extreme boss |
| Party + 4 | 160 | Extreme solo boss |

Prefer spending added budget on more creatures rather than one tougher foe — encounters read better when the enemy count is close to the PC count. Count troublesome terrain (climbing, difficult ground favouring ranged foes) against the budget if it materially raises the threat.

### Quick Groups (party of four)

- **Boss and Lackeys** (120): one Party + 2, four Party − 4
- **Boss and Lieutenant** (120): one Party + 2, one Party level
- **Elite Enemies** (120): three Party level
- **Lieutenant and Lackeys** (80): one Party level, four Party − 4
- **Mated Pair** (80): two Party level
- **Troop** (80): one Party level, two Party − 2
- **Mook Squad** (60): six Party − 4

## Rest and Recovery

A full **8 hours of rest** per 24-hour period grants:

- **HP recovered** = Constitution modifier (minimum 1) × character level. Halve it (min 1 HP) for rest without shelter or comfort.
- Removes the **fatigued** condition (unless the character slept in armour, which itself causes fatigue).
- Reduces **doomed** and **drained** severity by 1.
- Spellcasters regain daily spells.

Going more than 16 hours without sleep makes a character fatigued. Faster healing comes from **long-term rest**, a downtime activity that can't be used during exploration.

### Watches and Rest

Resting in the field means rotating watches. Larger groups need less personal watch time.

| Group Size | Total Time | Each Watch |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 2 | 16 h | 8 h |
| 3 | 12 h | 4 h |
| 4 | 10 h 40 m | 2 h 40 m |

### Treat Wounds

Between fights, a character trained in Medicine can **Treat Wounds** on an ally to restore HP (a discrete exploration activity a PC can drop out of travel to perform).

## Hero Points

- Every PC starts each session with **1 Hero Point**; remind players at the top of the session.
- A Hero Point can be spent to reroll a check, or all of a PC's points spent at once to avoid death.
- Award more during play for heroics, clever plays, and dramatic moments — typically alongside a moderate or major accomplishment. Aim for a handful over a session; a physical token helps you remember.

## Experience Points

Give XP for defeating or bypassing encounters (award the planned XP if the party cleverly avoids a fight) plus **accomplishment XP** for story milestones. In a typical session expect several minor awards, one or two moderate awards, and at most one major award. Give at least some accomplishment XP every session, and be consistent about what earns it.

## Victory Points (Subsystem Sketch)

Victory Points (VP) track progress on high-stakes challenges outside combat — infiltrations, influence campaigns, chases, research. Rename them to fit the fiction (Influence Points, Awareness Points, etc.).

- **Accumulating:** PCs gather VP toward a threshold, or as many as possible before a deadline, then compare against ranked tiers of outcomes. The default per-check scale: critical success +2 VP, success +1, failure 0, critical failure −1.
- **Diminishing:** PCs start with VP and try not to lose them (racing a timer, minimising damage); running dry triggers a bad event.
- **Multiple pools:** track two opposed totals at once — e.g. Infiltration Points the PCs earn versus Awareness Points their enemies accrue.

Set the DCs for obstacles in advance using the normal DC rules; improvise the rest. Built-in subsystems (influence, infiltration, chases, research, reputation, duels, hexploration) are all VP frameworks under different names.

## Session Flow Checklist

### Open

1. Establish where the PCs are and whether they had time to rest since last session.
2. Remind each player they hold 1 Hero Point.
3. Reaffirm goals — what the group wants to accomplish this session.
4. Pick a starting mode and prompt the action (a question to one PC, a rolled initiative, or an evocative scene).

### Run

1. Manage mode switches; call for initiative the instant stakes spike.
2. Set DCs on the fly from the simple or level-based tables; adjust for rarity and difficulty.
3. Keep encounter pace up: announce who's up and who's on deck, show consequences immediately.
4. Check in with quiet players; give every PC a spotlight beat.
5. Track XP, treasure handed out, and Hero Points awarded.

### Close

1. Award accomplishment XP and note who levels.
2. Distribute treasure earned; log currency and items.
3. Recap outstanding threads and unresolved subsystem points.
4. End on a hook or clear next goal.
