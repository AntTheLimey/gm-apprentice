# Forged in the Dark -- Cohorts

*GM activities: Run, Track, Prep*

**Related FitD files:**
- Crew types and upgrades: `systems/fitd/crew-types.md`
- Session procedures (downtime, group actions): `systems/fitd/session-procedures.md`
- Faction mechanics (Tier, scale): `systems/fitd/factions.md`

A cohort is a gang or expert NPC who works for your crew.
Recruiting a new cohort costs two crew upgrade boxes.

## Gangs

Choose a gang type that defines their skills and methods:

| Type | Members |
|------|---------|
| **Adepts** | Scholars, tinkerers, occultists, chemists |
| **Rooks** | Con artists, spies, socialites |
| **Rovers** | Sailors, carriage drivers, deathlands scavengers |
| **Skulks** | Scouts, infiltrators, thieves |
| **Thugs** | Killers, brawlers, roustabouts |

A gang's **quality** and **scale** both equal your crew's Tier.
When the gang acts within their type, roll their full quality.
Actions outside their type use quality zero.

### Adding Types

You can add a second type to a gang by spending two crew
upgrades (max two types per cohort). This broadens the gang's
competence without recruiting a new cohort.

### Elite Upgrades

Some crew upgrades grant "Elite [Type]" — cohorts of that type
get +1d to quality rolls. This stacks with Tier improvements.

## Experts

An expert is a single skilled NPC: a doctor, investigator,
occultist, assassin, spy, or similar specialist.

- **Quality**: crew Tier + 1 (experts are more skilled than
  rank-and-file gang members)
- **Scale**: always 0 (one person)

You may add a second area of expertise by spending two crew
upgrades, same as gangs.

## Edges and Flaws

Give each cohort one or two edges and an equal number of flaws.
These shape the cohort's personality and reliability.

**Edges:**
- **Fearsome** — enemies hesitate, civilians flee
- **Independent** — can handle tasks without direct oversight
- **Loyal** — won't betray the crew, even under pressure
- **Tenacious** — don't give up, push through setbacks

**Flaws:**
- **Principled** — have moral limits, may refuse certain jobs
- **Savage** — prone to excessive violence, hard to restrain
- **Unreliable** — may not show up, may cut corners
- **Wild** — unpredictable, may act on impulse

## Cohort Harm

Cohorts take harm like PCs, but with their own track:

| Level | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| **Weakened** | Reduced effect |
| **Impaired** | -1d to quality rolls |
| **Broken** | Can't do anything until recovered |
| **Dead** | Destroyed. Replace at cost: Tier + 2 coin plus two downtime activities |

All cohorts heal one level during downtime automatically. A PC
spending a downtime activity to help the cohort removes two
levels instead.

## Using a Cohort

**Unsupervised:** When you send a cohort to achieve a goal
without PC oversight, roll their quality (a fortune roll) to
see how it goes. The GM judges position and effect based on
the situation and the opposition's Tier.

**Supervised — group action:** A PC can oversee the cohort by
leading a group action:
- **Command** if you direct their efforts from a position of
  authority
- The **appropriate action** if you participate alongside them

The PC rolls their action, the cohort rolls its quality, and
you take the best result (standard group action rules). The
leader takes stress for each roll of 1-3.

### Position and Effect

When a cohort faces opposition:
- **Higher Tier than opposition:** dominant position, greater
  effect
- **Same Tier:** standard situation
- **Lower Tier:** worse position, reduced effect

These factors combine with the fictional circumstances to set
the final position and effect as usual.

### Scale in Conflict

When gangs clash, compare scale (determined by Tier). A gang
with greater scale has a significant advantage — the smaller
group needs potency, special abilities, or clever tactics to
even the odds. A single PC facing a gang is at severe
disadvantage without abilities like "Not to be Trifled With."
