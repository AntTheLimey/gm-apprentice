## Consequence Fiction

When a PC gets a partial success (4-5) or fails (1-3), the GM
inflicts consequences. The five consequence types and how they
look in play:

### Reduced Effect

The action works, but not as well as hoped. The outcome is
diminished.

- A PC tries to intimidate a suspect, but the target only
  gives up one piece of information instead of the full story
- A lock is picked, but it takes longer than expected — the
  patrol is closer now
- A fight is won, but the crew's reputation benefit is less
  than anticipated

### Complication

A new problem arises or an existing situation worsens. Often
represented by ticking a clock.

- During a heist, the building's arcane alarm activates — start
  a "Guards Alerted" clock
- A social maneuver succeeds, but the target mentions the
  encounter to a rival faction
- The crew's escape route is blocked by an unexpected
  collapse — find another way out

### Lost Opportunity

The window for this particular approach closes. The PC must
try a different tactic or abandon the goal.

- The fence's ship has sailed — literally. That buyer is no
  longer available for this score
- The distraction worked, but the moment to slip past has
  already gone; the guards have regrouped
- The occult text crumbles when touched — this copy is
  destroyed, find another source

### Worse Position

The fiction shifts so the PC's next action starts from a worse
stance. Controlled becomes risky, risky becomes desperate.

- A failed stealth attempt doesn't get you caught, but now
  the guards are on alert — your next move is desperate
- Your cover story holds for now, but the target is suspicious
  — further deception is risky instead of controlled
- You parry the first blow, but your opponent has you backed
  against a wall — your position worsens

### Harm

Physical or mental damage to the PC. Ranges from bruises to
life-threatening.

- **Level 1** (less severe): Bruised ribs, rattled nerves,
  twisted ankle
- **Level 2** (moderate): Broken arm, concussion, deep
  laceration
- **Level 3** (severe): Shattered leg, impaled, brain damage
- **Level 4** (fatal): Electrocuted, gutted, skull crushed
  (requires immediate resistance or death)

### Choosing Consequences

Match the consequence to the fiction. A failed Prowl through
a guarded corridor naturally suggests worse position or a
complication (guards alerted), not reduced effect. A partial
success in combat naturally suggests harm. Let the fictional
situation guide which type fits.

When in doubt, **complication** (tick a clock) is the most
versatile — it creates forward momentum and gives the crew
something to deal with without immediately punishing them.

## Devil's Bargain Design

A devil's bargain offers the player +1d to their roll in
exchange for accepting a complication — regardless of the
roll's outcome. The bargain is accepted before the roll and
cannot be resisted afterward.

### What Makes a Good Bargain

A bargain should be **meaningful but not crippling**. It
creates an interesting new thread, not a dead end. The
complication should feel like a fair trade, something the
player genuinely weighs rather than automatically accepts or
rejects.

### Example Bargains by Situation

**Stealth:**
- You get past the guards, but you leave a distinctive mark
  that an investigator could trace back to you
- You find the hidden entrance, but you overhear something
  you wish you hadn't — take stress

**Social:**
- You charm the target, but you inadvertently promise a favour
  that will be called in later
- You get the information, but your contact now knows what
  you're planning and might sell that to someone else

**Combat:**
- You strike true, but your weapon is damaged (reduced effect
  next time)
- You gain the upper hand, but a bystander is endangered by
  the chaos

**Occult:**
- The ritual works, but something in the ghost field notices
  you — start a clock
- You compel the spirit, but it marks you — supernatural
  entities will sense you more easily for a while

**Downtime:**
- Your long-term project advances an extra tick, but you
  attract unwanted attention from a faction
- You recover from harm faster, but you owe a debt to whoever
  helped you

### Bargains vs Pushing

Players may push themselves (+1d for 2 stress) or accept a
devil's bargain (+1d for a complication). They can do both on
the same roll. When deciding whether to offer a bargain, ask:
would this complication create an interesting situation? If
yes, offer it. If the complication would just be annoying
without story value, let the player push instead.

## Position and Effect in Practice

### Setting Position

Position reflects the PC's fictional stance — how exposed
they are to danger.

| Position | When it applies |
|----------|----------------|
| **Controlled** | PC has clear advantage, the worst outcome is a minor setback. Rarely used during scores — most score actions are at least risky. |
| **Risky** | The default. PC faces real danger, things could go either way. If you're unsure, it's risky. |
| **Desperate** | PC is in serious trouble. Failure means severe consequences. More XP on a desperate roll. |

**Common mistakes:**
- Setting everything to risky by default without considering
  fiction — a thief picking a simple lock in an empty room
  is controlled, not risky
- Never using desperate — if the PC is cornered, outmatched,
  or acting against a much higher Tier opponent, that's
  desperate
- Forgetting that position can change mid-scene as the
  fiction evolves

### Setting Effect

Effect reflects how much impact the action will have on
success.

| Effect | When it applies |
|--------|----------------|
| **Great** | PC has dominant advantage — superior tools, potency, scale, or fictional positioning |
| **Standard** | The default. PC acts on roughly equal footing with the obstacle |
| **Limited** | PC is at disadvantage — outmatched in quality, scale, or approach |

**The dominant factor:** When setting effect, identify the
single most important factor. Is the PC's quality/Tier
outmatched? Do they have potency (supernatural advantage,
fine equipment, special ability)? Is their scale appropriate?
The dominant factor sets the baseline, then other factors
nudge up or down.

### Trading Position for Effect

The player may propose a trade before rolling:
- **Take worse position for better effect** — "I'll charge
  in recklessly (desperate) to do more damage (great effect)"
- **Take better position for worse effect** — "I'll be
  cautious (controlled) but accept that I won't accomplish
  much (limited effect)"

This trade is a negotiation between player and GM. Both must
agree the fiction supports it.

### The 3×3 Matrix

Position and effect combine to define the stakes:

| | Limited effect | Standard effect | Great effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Controlled** | Low stakes: small gain, small risk | Moderate gain, manageable risk | Big gain, manageable risk |
| **Risky** | Small gain, real danger | The default: meaningful gain, real danger | Big gain, real danger |
| **Desperate** | Small gain, severe danger | Meaningful gain, severe danger (but more XP) | Big gain, severe danger (but more XP) |

A desperate action with limited effect is a bad deal — the PC
risks severe consequences for minimal gain. When this comes
up, make sure the player knows the stakes before they commit.
They might choose a different approach.

## Clock Patterns

Progress clocks appear throughout the session cycle —
tracking score objectives, faction projects, long-term
investigations, and looming threats. This section covers
practical usage; see `session-procedures.md` for the clock
type reference table.

### Layering Clocks for Tension

The most dramatic moments often involve multiple clocks
running simultaneously:

- **Progress + danger:** The crew works a "Crack the Vault"
  clock while a "Guards Close In" clock ticks alongside.
  Every action advances one or both.
- **Racing clocks:** Two factions (or a faction and the crew)
  pursue competing goals. First to fill their clock wins.
  Great for faction turns that intersect with crew interests.
- **Nested clocks:** Completing one clock enables the next.
  "Infiltrate the Compound" must fill before "Sabotage the
  Machine" can begin.

### Clock Sizing

| Segments | When to use |
|----------|-------------|
| 4 | Simple obstacle, one or two rolls to overcome |
| 6 | Standard challenge, several rolls needed |
| 8 | Complex, extended effort — long-term projects, major faction goals |
| 10-12 | Rare, campaign-spanning ambitions |

If you find a 4-clock filling in one roll every time, the
obstacle probably didn't need a clock — just resolve it with
a single action roll.

### Common Anti-Patterns

- **Too many active clocks:** If you're tracking more than 4-5
  clocks in a single score, some of them aren't doing real
  work. Combine or drop the ones that aren't creating
  decisions.
- **Clocks that don't matter:** A clock with no stakes —
  nothing interesting happens when it fills — shouldn't exist.
  Every clock should answer "what happens when this fills?"
- **Forgotten danger clocks:** If a danger clock exists, tick
  it. Failing to advance danger clocks removes the tension
  they're supposed to create. If you forgot about a danger
  clock, it should probably be ticking faster.
- **Clocks as HP bars:** Clocks track progress toward a
  fictional outcome, not hit points. "Defeat the guards"
  isn't a 6-clock — it's an action roll or series of action
  rolls. Save clocks for objectives that unfold over time.
