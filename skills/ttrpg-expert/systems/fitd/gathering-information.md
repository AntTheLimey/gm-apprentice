# Forged in the Dark -- Gathering Information

*GM activities: Run, Prep*

**Related FitD files:**
- Core mechanics (actions, dice): `systems/fitd/mechanics.md`
- Session procedures (free play phase): `systems/fitd/session-procedures.md`
- Position and effect: `systems/fitd/rules-reference.md`

Information flow from the GM to the players is central to
play. By default, the GM describes what characters perceive,
suspect, and intuit. When players want to know something
specific, they gather information.

## The Basics

When a PC wants to learn something about the fictional world,
the GM asks how the character goes about it (or how they
learned it in the past). The answer determines what happens:

| Situation | Resolution |
|-----------|------------|
| Common knowledge | GM simply answers |
| No obstacle but not common knowledge | Fortune roll — quality of info depends on result |
| An obstacle blocks the answer | Action roll — consequences possible |

Each attempt to gather information takes time. The situation
may allow multiple tries, or the opportunity may be fleeting
(one chance only).

The GM always answers honestly, but the **level of detail**
scales with the **effect level** of the roll.

## Effect Levels on Information

| Effect | What you learn |
|--------|---------------|
| **Great** | Exceptional detail. Complete answer, possibly expanding into related areas or revealing more than expected |
| **Standard** | Good detail. Solid answer with room for follow-up questions |
| **Limited** | Incomplete or partial information. More gathering needed for the full picture |

## Which Actions Apply

Any of the 12 actions can be used to gather information,
depending on the fiction. The most common approaches:

### Attune
Reach into the ghost field to sense recent spirit activity,
detect supernatural presences, or perceive things beyond
normal sight.

*Example questions:* Have any spirits been active here
recently? How can I locate the source of this supernatural
disturbance? What should I be worried about in the ghost
field?

### Command
Pressure someone into telling you what they know. Works on
targets who can't or won't resist your authority.

*Example questions:* What's really going on here? Who is this
person working for? What are they hiding?

### Consort
Leverage your social network — friends, contacts, fellow
criminals — to learn secrets about enemies, rivals, or
potential allies.

*Example questions:* What do they intend to do? What might I
suspect about their true motives? How can I find leverage to
use against them?

### Hunt
Track someone across the city, follow a courier, or trace a
trail to its source.

*Example questions:* Where does the trail lead? Who's
receiving these deliveries? How can I discover more about
this operation?

### Study
Research a topic through books, documents, or careful analysis.
Also used to read a person's intentions and feelings.

*Example questions:* How can I neutralize these protective
wards? Will anyone notice if I tamper with them? What are
they really feeling? How could I earn their trust?

### Survey
Observe a location, scan a room, or read a charged situation
to understand what's happening and anticipate what comes next.

*Example questions:* What's a good entry point? What's the
main danger here? Are they about to attack? What's the
security like?

### Sway
Work someone socially — at a party, in a meeting, through
flattery or misdirection — to get them to reveal information
they'd normally keep private.

*Example questions:* What are their future plans? How can I
position myself as a useful ally? What have they been doing
lately? Who are their associates?

## Positioning Before Gathering

Sometimes you need to maneuver into position before you can
gather information. For example, you might need to Prowl to a
hiding spot before you can Study a secret meeting, or Consort
your way into a party before you can Survey the host's manor.

The GM may call for a separate action roll for the positioning
step, with its own consequences.

## Investigation as Long-Term Project

Some questions are too complex for a single roll. When a PC
wants to uncover something that requires sustained effort —
mapping a smuggling network, tracing the source of a
conspiracy, researching an ancient mystery — the GM calls for
a **long-term project** worked during downtime.

Track the investigation with a **progress clock** (typically
8 segments). Once filled, the PC has the evidence to ask
several questions about the subject as if they had **great
effect**.

## GM Guidance

### When to Volunteer Information

If the PCs are in a position to notice something, tell them.
Don't make players roll to notice obvious things. The gather
information mechanic is for actively seeking answers, not for
passive perception.

### Calibrating Disclosure

The goal is to give players enough information to make
interesting decisions, not to gate all knowledge behind rolls.
When in doubt:

- **Lean generous with context** — players need to understand
  the fictional situation to make meaningful choices
- **Roll for specifics** — the question isn't "do you notice
  the guards" but "how many guards, and what's their routine?"
- **Fortune roll for quality** — when there's no obstacle but
  the depth of knowledge varies

### Information Feeds Planning

Gathering information during free play directly shapes the
score. The details players learn — guard schedules, building
layouts, faction rivalries, NPC vulnerabilities — become the
raw material for their plan. Richer information gathering
leads to more creative and informed score planning.
