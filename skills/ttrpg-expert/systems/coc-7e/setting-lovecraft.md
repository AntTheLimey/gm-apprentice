# Call of Cthulhu 7e — Lovecraftian Setting Reference

Locations, entities, and themes from H.P. Lovecraft's own
published fiction. Used during prep (setting research) and
world-building (developing atmosphere and plot hooks).

## Lovecraft's New England

Lovecraft set most of his fiction in a fictionalised version
of New England, drawing on the actual geography, architecture,
and social character of Massachusetts and its surroundings.
The following locations are his creations, described as he
wrote them.

### Arkham, Massachusetts
**Sources**: "The Picture in the House" (1921), "Herbert
West—Reanimator" (1922), "The Unnamable" (1925), "The
Colour Out of Space" (1927), many others.

A decaying New England university town modelled on Salem and
the towns of the Miskatonic River valley. Home to Miskatonic
University, whose library holds rare and dangerous volumes.
Arkham is characterised by gambrel-roofed colonial houses,
narrow streets, ancient elms, and an atmosphere of intellectual
ambition shadowed by dark history. The countryside around
Arkham is dotted with abandoned farms and blasted heaths
where the land itself seems corrupted.

**Key features**: Miskatonic University and its library
(repository of forbidden texts), old colonial architecture,
the Miskatonic River, surrounding farmland with a history of
strange events.

### Dunwich, Massachusetts
**Source**: "The Dunwich Horror" (1929).

An isolated, decadent village in the hills west of Arkham.
Dunwich is a community in decline: once-proud families have
degenerated through isolation, poverty, and inbreeding. The
landscape is dominated by round-topped hills with standing
stones on their summits, where strange sounds and lights
appear on certain nights. The Whateley family of Dunwich
conducted rituals to summon Yog-Sothoth, producing offspring
that were partly human and partly something else entirely.

**Key features**: Round-topped hills with standing stones,
the Whateley farmhouse, decaying village, whippoorwills that
sing when someone is about to die, a population shunned by
neighbouring towns.

### Innsmouth, Massachusetts
**Source**: "The Shadow over Innsmouth" (1931 — written 1927).

A crumbling seaport on the Massachusetts coast, almost
entirely abandoned by normal commerce. The remaining
inhabitants bear a distinctive physical appearance — bulging
eyes, narrow heads, rough scaly skin — known as "the Innsmouth
look." Captain Obed Marsh brought back a cult of Dagon worship
from the South Seas, and the town's people intermarried with
Deep Ones in exchange for gold and plentiful fishing. The
Esoteric Order of Dagon controls the town. Beneath Devil Reef
offshore lies a city of the Deep Ones.

**Key features**: The Esoteric Order of Dagon, Devil Reef,
Marsh family, fish-processing economy, the Innsmouth look
(gradual transformation), the Gilman House hotel, abandoned
mill district.

### Kingsport, Massachusetts
**Sources**: "The Festival" (1925), "The Strange High House
in the Mist" (1931 — written 1926).

An ancient, fog-shrouded seaport clinging to sheer cliffs.
Kingsport has an otherworldly quality even before any
supernatural elements are introduced — its steep streets,
ancient buildings, and perpetual sea-mist make it feel
timeless and slightly unreal. The town harbours secret
traditions and festivals dating back centuries, connected to
pre-Christian rites. High on the cliffs above the town sits
a strange house perpetually wreathed in mist, from which
none return unchanged.

**Key features**: Cliff-side architecture, perpetual fog,
ancient festivals and secret rites, the Strange High House
in the Mist, a sense of timelessness.

## Beyond New England

### R'lyeh
**Source**: "The Call of Cthulhu" (1928).

A sunken city in the South Pacific (S. Latitude 47 9',
W. Longitude 126 43') where great Cthulhu lies dead-dreaming.
The city's geometry is described as "abnormal, non-Euclidean"
— angles that seem simultaneously acute and obtuse, surfaces
that appear concave and convex at once. The architecture
comprises massive stone blocks of greenish hue, covered in
hieroglyphs. When R'lyeh briefly rises from the ocean, the
sheer wrongness of its geometry causes madness in those who
behold it. A massive carved door "which they knew must be a
door because of the ornate lintel" serves as the entrance
to Cthulhu's tomb.

**Key features**: Non-Euclidean architecture, colossal scale,
greenish stone, hieroglyphic carvings, Cthulhu's tomb, the
geometry of madness.

### The Plateau of Leng
**Sources**: "The Hound" (1924), "The Dream-Quest of Unknown
Kadath" (1943 — written 1927).

A high, desolate plateau that exists in both the waking world
and the Dreamlands. In the waking world it is associated with
Central Asia; in the Dreamlands it is a cold, wind-swept
wasteland inhabited by satyr-like beings and presided over
by a High Priest Not to Be Described. Leng is a place of
ancient evil, where reality thins and the boundaries between
worlds blur.

**Key features**: Desolate high plateau, cold and wind-swept,
the monastery of the High Priest, satyr-like inhabitants,
stone villages, a place where dimensions overlap.

### Kadath (The Unknown)
**Source**: "The Dream-Quest of Unknown Kadath" (1943 — written
1927).

The home of the Great Ones (Earth's gods) in the cold waste
beyond the Dreamlands. Kadath is hidden and its location
unknown even to the gods themselves. It is reached through
the Dreamlands by passing through increasingly alien
landscapes. The castle upon Kadath is of onyx, standing in
eternal twilight. Nyarlathotep guards the approach and uses
trickery to prevent seekers from reaching it.

**Key features**: The onyx castle, eternal twilight, the cold
waste, guarded by Nyarlathotep, reachable only through the
Dreamlands.

### Antarctica
**Source**: "At the Mountains of Madness" (1936 — written 1931).

Beyond the known mountain ranges of Antarctica lies a vast
plateau of ancient ruins — the remains of a city built by
the Elder Things millions of years before humanity evolved.
The city's walls are covered in bas-reliefs depicting the
Elder Things' history: their arrival on Earth, their creation
of the shoggoths, their wars with other alien races, and
their slow decline. Beneath the ruins, tunnels lead to a
lightless abyss where shoggoths still lurk.

**Key features**: Mountain ranges beyond the known, cyclopean
ruins, bas-relief murals telling pre-human history, tunnels
to the abyss, shoggoths.

## Cosmic Entities as Lovecraft Described Them

Lovecraft's entities are not villains in any conventional
sense. They are beings of such vast scale and alien nature
that humanity is simply irrelevant to them. They do not hate
us; they do not notice us.

**Cthulhu** — Dead but dreaming in sunken R'lyeh. Sends
dreams to sensitive minds. A being of mountainous size with
an octopoid head, scaly body, and wings.
("The Call of Cthulhu," 1928)

**Azathoth** — The blind idiot god at the centre of ultimate
chaos, lulled by the thin monotonous piping of a demonic
flute. All of reality dances to its mindless rhythm.
("Azathoth," 1922; "The Dream-Quest of Unknown Kadath," 1927)

**Nyarlathotep** — The Crawling Chaos, messenger and soul of
the Outer Gods. Takes many forms. Uniquely among cosmic
entities, he walks among and interacts with humanity, often
with apparent malice or dark humour.
("Nyarlathotep," 1920; "The Dream-Quest of Unknown Kadath,"
1927)

**Yog-Sothoth** — Coterminous with all time and space. The
gate, the key, and the guardian of the gate. Can grant
forbidden knowledge. Father of the Whateley twins.
("The Dunwich Horror," 1929)

**Shub-Niggurath** — The Black Goat of the Woods with a
Thousand Young. Associated with fertility, forests, and
dark worship.
(Referenced in many stories; never directly depicted)

**Dagon** — A titanic aquatic being worshipped by the Deep
Ones. Associated with the degeneration of coastal communities
and the lure of the deep ocean.
("Dagon," 1919)

## Core Themes of Lovecraftian Horror

### Cosmic Indifference
The universe is vast, ancient, and utterly indifferent to
human existence. There is no benevolent creator, no cosmic
justice, no meaning inherent in the arrangement of things.
Humanity exists in a brief, fragile moment between eternities
of inhuman activity.

### Forbidden Knowledge
The truth about the universe is not liberating — it is
destructive. Those who learn too much about the nature of
reality lose their ability to function within it. Knowledge
of the Mythos erodes sanity. Every tome read, every ritual
witnessed, every entity glimpsed takes something from the
investigator that cannot be restored.

### Deep Time
Human civilisation is a momentary flicker. Earth has been
inhabited by intelligent species for hundreds of millions of
years. Their ruins lie beneath our cities, beneath the ocean,
beneath the Antarctic ice. We are neither the first nor the
most significant inhabitants of our own planet.

### Hereditary Corruption
Bloodlines carry hidden taint. The people of Innsmouth bear
the mark of their Deep One ancestry. The Whateley family's
dealings with Yog-Sothoth produce inhuman offspring. One's
own body and heritage can be a source of horror.

### The Fragility of Civilisation
The veneer of rationality and social order is thin.
Isolation, ancestral secrets, and encounters with the
unknown can strip it away, revealing something primal and
terrifying beneath.

### The Unknowable
The most merciful thing in the world, Lovecraft wrote, is
the inability of the human mind to correlate all its
contents. The horror is not in what is known, but in the
implication that there are things fundamentally beyond human
comprehension — and that some of those things are aware of us.
