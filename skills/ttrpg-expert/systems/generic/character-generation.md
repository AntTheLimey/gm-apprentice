# Generic System -- Character Generation

## Overview

Universal character creation guidance for any tabletop RPG. When
the user's system doesn't have a dedicated subfolder, use these
principles to guide them through building a character. The
specific mechanics change per system, but the creative process
is remarkably consistent.

## Concept First

Every system benefits from a clear character concept before
touching mechanics. A concept is a one- or two-sentence pitch:

- "A disgraced knight seeking redemption through anonymous deeds."
- "A con artist who genuinely believes their own lies."
- "A scholar who accidentally opened something they can't close."

The concept doesn't need mechanical detail. It needs a person
with a motivation and a contradiction.

## The Three-Question Character Pitch

Before building anything on paper, answer three questions:

1. **Who are you?** A brief identity: role, background, and
   personality in a sentence or two.
2. **What do you want?** A concrete goal that drives action.
   Not "be a hero" but "find my missing sister" or "prove I
   deserve the title they stripped from me."
3. **Why are you with this group?** The most important question.
   A character who has no reason to work with the party is a
   problem, not a concept. Build the connection in from the start.

## Common Building Blocks

Most RPGs construct characters from some combination of these
elements. Not every system uses all of them:

| Element | What It Covers | Examples |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Attributes / Stats | Raw capabilities (strength, intelligence, etc.) | D&D ability scores, PbtA stats, GURPS attributes |
| Skills / Abilities | Trained competencies | Percentile skills, skill ranks, action ratings |
| Class / Playbook / Archetype | A package of abilities defining a role | D&D classes, PbtA playbooks, Warhammer careers |
| Background / Origin | Where the character comes from | D&D backgrounds, CoC occupations, lifepath results |
| Equipment / Loadout | Starting gear and resources | Weapon lists, loadout slots, starting packages |
| Special Powers / Features | Unique abilities beyond basic skills | Spells, mutations, cyberware, stunts, moves |

## The Three Creation Paradigms

| Paradigm | How It Works | Systems |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Point-buy** | Spend a budget of points across attributes, skills, advantages. Trade-offs are explicit. | GURPS, Hero System, Shadowrun |
| **Random generation** | Roll dice for attributes, sometimes for background or career. Embraces chance. | Classic D&D, Traveller, Warhammer Fantasy |
| **Template / class-based** | Choose a package that determines most of your mechanical identity. Customize within it. | D&D 5e, PbtA playbooks, Savage Worlds edges |

Many modern systems blend these: choose a class, then spend
points within it, or roll attributes then pick a template.

## Mechanical Identity vs Narrative Identity

Two complementary layers define every character:

- **Mechanical identity** -- what the numbers say you CAN do.
  Your best stats, your key abilities, your combat role. This
  is what matters when dice hit the table.
- **Narrative identity** -- what the fiction says you WILL do.
  Your backstory, your personality, your relationships. This
  is what matters between rolls.

A well-built character has both, and they reinforce each other.
A cunning rogue should have the stats to be cunning AND a reason
in the fiction to be roguish.

**Watch for mismatches.** If a player describes a charming
diplomat but puts all their points into combat stats, help them
reconcile the two. Either adjust the concept or the numbers.

## Session Zero Guidance

Session zero is the collaborative process of building characters
and setting expectations together. Universal principles:

### Tone Alignment
- What genre and tone is the campaign? Horror, heroic, gritty,
  comedic? Characters should match.
- What content is off-limits? Establish safety tools (X-card,
  lines/veils, Script Change) before play begins.

### Party Composition
- Do the characters cover different niches? Not every system
  requires mechanical balance, but every group benefits from
  characters who are good at different things.
- Do the characters have reasons to work together? Build
  connections during session zero, not after.

### Shared History
- Consider having characters who already know each other.
  Relationships established before play starts generate better
  scenes than strangers meeting in a tavern.
- Some systems formalize this (Dungeon World bonds, Fiasco
  relationships, FitD crew creation). If yours doesn't, do it
  informally.

## When You Don't Know the System

If the user asks for character creation help in an unsupported
system:

1. Ask what character creation looks like: "Is it point-buy,
   random rolls, or template-based? What choices do you make?"
2. Guide the creative process (concept, motivation, group fit)
   regardless of mechanical details.
3. If they share their system's building blocks, help them
   optimize toward their concept.
4. Reference the three-question pitch and session zero guidance
   above -- these work in every system.
