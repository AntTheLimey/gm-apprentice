# Generic System -- Session Procedures

## Overview

Universal session-running guidance that works regardless of game
system. These are the structural patterns that underlie every
good RPG session. For detailed frameworks, see the system-agnostic
files at the ttrpg-expert root: `session-planner.md`,
`gm-session-patterns.md`, and `active-play-management.md`.

## Session Structure

Most good sessions follow a three-act shape, whether or not the
GM plans it that way:

### Act 1: Opening Hook (10-15 minutes)
- **Recap:** Have a player summarize last session. This reveals
  what they found important, which may differ from what you
  planned.
- **Strong start:** Open with action, tension, or a decision --
  not logistics. "You're mid-chase" beats "you wake up at the
  inn."
- **Reestablish stakes:** Remind the group what's at risk and
  what they're trying to accomplish.

### Act 2: Rising Tension (60-90 minutes)
- **The meat of the session.** Exploration, investigation,
  social encounters, combat, or any combination.
- **Escalate.** Each scene should raise the stakes or reveal
  new information. If things feel flat, introduce a complication.
- **Vary the pace.** Alternate between high-tension and
  low-tension scenes. A combat followed by a quiet character
  moment followed by a discovery hits harder than three combats
  in a row.

### Act 3: Climax and Resolution (15-30 minutes)
- **Deliver the payoff.** The boss fight, the revelation, the
  confrontation, the escape.
- **Don't rush the ending.** Give players a moment to react and
  process what happened.
- **Set up next session.** Plant a hook, leave a thread
  dangling, or end on a cliffhanger.

## Pacing

### When to Speed Up
- Travel between known locations (unless travel IS the content).
- Shopping, bookkeeping, and logistics.
- Scenes where nothing is at stake.
- Combat rounds where the outcome is already decided.

### When to Slow Down
- Dramatic character moments and emotional beats.
- Complex tactical decisions in combat.
- Investigation scenes where players are piecing things together.
- Any moment where a player is clearly invested.

### The Rule of "Is This Fun Right Now?"
If a scene is dragging, cut to the next interesting thing. If
players are deeply engaged, let the scene breathe even if it
runs long. The session plan serves the table, not the other way
around.

## Encounter Design (System-Agnostic)

Good encounters share four universal properties regardless of
system:

1. **Clear objective.** The players know what they're trying to
   accomplish (even if the path is unclear).
2. **Interesting environment.** The space itself creates tactical
   or narrative options -- terrain, lighting, hazards, bystanders.
3. **Meaningful stakes.** Something is gained or lost based on the
   outcome. No encounter should feel like busywork.
4. **Multiple approaches.** Combat, stealth, negotiation,
   cleverness -- at least two viable paths to resolution.

### The Three-Encounter Session
A reliable structure: one social encounter, one exploration or
investigation encounter, one combat or action encounter. Vary the
order. Not every session needs all three, but this mix keeps
things dynamic.

## Improvisation Toolkit

### The Yes-And / Yes-But Framework
- **Yes, and...** -- the action succeeds and something extra
  happens. Use when you want momentum.
- **Yes, but...** -- the action succeeds with a complication.
  The default for interesting results.
- **No, but...** -- the action fails but something useful happens
  anyway. Softens failure.
- **No, and...** -- the action fails and things get worse. Use
  sparingly for dramatic escalation.

### Instant NPC Generation
When you need an NPC on the fly: pick a **want** (what do they
need right now?), a **quirk** (one memorable trait), and an
**obstacle** (what's in their way). See `npc-generation.md` for
detailed frameworks.

### Random Complications
When a scene stalls, introduce a complication: a timer starts,
a new faction arrives, the environment changes, an ally is
compromised, or a secret is revealed. See `random-generation.md`
and `content-generation.md` for tables and generators.

## Ending a Session

### Natural Stopping Points
- After a major combat or encounter resolves.
- When the group reaches a safe location.
- After a major revelation or plot twist.
- At a decision point ("you have three leads -- which do you
  follow?").

### The Cliffhanger
End mid-action or mid-revelation when you can. "The door opens,
and behind it you see --" gets people back to the table faster
than a tidy conclusion.

### Advancement and Rewards
- **XP-based systems:** Award XP at session end and let players
  level up between sessions (avoids mid-session bookkeeping).
- **Milestone systems:** Advance at narrative milestones, not
  session boundaries.
- **Session-based advancement:** Some systems advance per session
  played (Blades in the Dark downtime, Torchbearer). Follow the
  system's rhythm.

## Arc Drivers

Every RPG system embeds story hooks in character mechanics.
Identify them during character creation and use them as arc
fuel:

**Built-in hooks by mechanic type:**
- **Flaws/disadvantages** (GURPS disads, D&D flaws, FitD
  trauma): situations that force the character to confront
  their weakness or pay its cost
- **Bonds/relationships** (backgrounds, contacts, dependents):
  NPCs the character cares about, placed under threat or
  given conflicting loyalties
- **Drives/beliefs** (FitD drives, Burning Wheel beliefs,
  D&D ideals): moral dilemmas that test stated values with
  no clean answer
- **Progression mechanics** (XP triggers, milestone goals,
  point-buy advancement): what the system rewards reveals
  what stories it wants told

**Per-session touchpoints (system-agnostic):**
- One scene activating a character's built-in hook
- One encounter showcasing a unique ability or skill
- One consequence from a prior decision (2-5 sessions delayed)
- One quiet moment for character expression (campfire,
  downtime, reflection)

Not every touchpoint needs to be dramatic. Some just signal
that the campaign remembers this character exists.
