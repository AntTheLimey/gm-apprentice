# Content Generation

Frameworks and procedures for generating campaign content
including scenes, locations, items, factions, creatures,
documents, and complete scenario outlines. This document covers
the generation philosophy, templates, and system-specific
guidance for producing ready-to-use TTRPG content.

## Generation Principles

### Situation Over Script

Generated content describes situations, not sequences. A
generated scene establishes who is present, what they want, what
the environment contains, and what complications exist. It never
prescribes what the PCs do or the order in which events unfold.

### Modular by Default

Each piece of generated content should function independently
and combine with other pieces. A generated location works
whether the PCs visit it in session 2 or session 12. A
generated NPC functions whether encountered at the market or
in the villain's lair. This modularity allows GMs to rearrange,
delay, or repurpose content as the campaign demands.

### Genre Fidelity

Generated content matches the tone and conventions of its game
system:

| System | Tone | Content Character |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| CoC 7e | Dread, mystery | Clues, slow reveals, mundane horror escalating to cosmic |
| D&D 5e | Heroic adventure | Bold challenges, clear stakes, rewarding exploration |
| GURPS 4e | Genre-flexible | Match the campaign's declared genre and lethality |
| FitD | Gritty heists | Obstacles, complications, faction entanglements |

### The Three Clue Principle in Generation

Whenever generating investigative content, every conclusion
the PCs need to reach must be supported by at least three
independently discoverable clues placed across different
nodes, NPCs, or locations. Never generate a mystery with a
single path to the answer.

## Scene Generation

### Scene Template

Every generated scene contains these components:

```
## [Scene Title]

**Type:** [Combat / Social / Investigation / Exploration /
  Chase / Downtime / Puzzle]

**Agenda:** [One sentence: what question does this scene
  answer or what situation does it resolve?]

**Opening:** [Read-aloud or sensory bullet points for the
  GM to paraphrase. Maximum 3 sentences. Objective sensory
  detail only.]

**Who Is Present:**
- [NPC/Creature]: [What they want, what they are doing]

**Environment:**
- [3 interactive elements the PCs can use or that affect play]
- [Lighting, sound, smell, temperature]

**Complications:**
- [2-3 things that can go wrong or change mid-scene]

**Possible Outcomes:**
- Success: [What changes in the world]
- Failure: [What changes in the world]
- Partial: [What changes in the world]
- Flight: [What happens if PCs disengage]

**Connections:** [Where this scene leads next, by outcome]

**System Notes:** [Relevant DCs, skill checks, SAN costs,
  position/effect, etc.]
```

### Scene Type Generation Guidelines

**Combat Scenes:**
- Define an objective beyond "defeat all enemies."
- Include 3 environmental elements (cover, hazards, terrain).
- Give enemies a goal and a retreat condition.
- Plan a mid-combat complication (reinforcements, terrain
  change, revelation, betrayal).
- Specify what happens on PC loss, flight, or negotiation.

**Social Scenes:**
- Establish the NPC's price (what they will accept) and line
  (what they will not cross).
- Allow multiple approaches: charm, intimidation, bribery,
  deception, shared interest appeal.
- Track disposition shifts across the conversation, not a
  single roll.
- Include partial success outcomes (some information, not all).

**Investigation Scenes:**
- State the conclusion PCs should reach.
- Place at least 3 clues pointing to that conclusion.
- Distribute clues across at least 2 different sources.
- Include 1 proactive clue that comes to the PCs.
- Prepare a fail-forward result for each skill-gated clue.
- Never include scenes that look investigative but reveal
  nothing.

**Exploration Scenes:**
- Use 3 distinctive sensory details per new area.
- Describe what is unusual, not the mundane.
- Montage through safe/empty areas.
- Slow down for discovery moments.
- Include something to interact with, not just observe.

**Chase Scenes:**
- Keep to 3-4 rounds (6-7 for extended chases).
- The environment is the adversary: obstacles, crowds, terrain.
- Give both sides meaningful choices each round.
- Use distance tracks or clocks to visualise the gap.

### Scene Framing in Generated Content

**Cut In Late:** Generated scenes begin at the moment of
decision, not during travel or preamble. If the scene is "the
PCs confront the suspect," it opens with the suspect already
in view, not with the PCs walking to the suspect's house.

**Deliver the Bang:** The first element of the scene demands a
response. A bang is a catalyst: the door is already kicked in
when the PCs arrive, the NPC collapses mid-sentence, the alarm
is already sounding.

**Exit Trigger:** Each generated scene includes a clear signal
for when it should end: the agenda is resolved, the information
is extracted, the threat is neutralised or escaped.

## Location Generation

### Location Template

```
## [Location Name]

**Type:** [Building / Outdoor / Underground / Vehicle / Other]

**Atmosphere:** [2-3 words capturing mood]

**Sensory Details:**
- Sight: [What stands out visually]
- Sound: [What the PCs hear]
- Smell/Touch: [What they smell or feel]

**Key Features:**
- [Feature 1]: [Description and interaction potential]
- [Feature 2]: [Description and interaction potential]
- [Feature 3]: [Description and interaction potential]

**Inhabitants:**
- [Who is here and what they are doing]

**Secrets:**
- [What is hidden and how it can be found]

**Connections:**
- [Where this location connects to other locations]
- [What clues point here from other nodes]
- [What clues here point to other nodes]

**Hazards:** [If any: traps, environmental dangers, creatures]

**System Notes:** [DCs, skill checks, environmental rules]
```

### Location Design Principles

- Every location should contain at least one thing to interact
  with, discover, or use tactically.
- Locations exist whether the PCs visit them or not. They have
  inhabitants with routines and events that occur on a timeline.
- Generate locations with multiple entry points and internal
  paths. Avoid single-corridor designs unless the bottleneck is
  intentional and interesting.
- Include vertical space (balconies, basements, rooftops) to
  create tactical depth.

## Item and Artifact Generation

### Item Template

```
## [Item Name]

**Type:** [Weapon / Armor / Tool / Tome / Artifact / Mundane]

**Description:** [Physical appearance and distinctive features]

**Origin:** [Where it came from and who made it]

**Properties:**
- [Mechanical effect 1]
- [Mechanical effect 2, if any]

**History:** [Brief narrative significance]

**Current Location:** [Where it is now]

**Hooks:** [Why PCs might want it or encounter it]

**System Notes:** [Full mechanical details per game system]
```

### Item Design Principles

- Items should create story opportunities, not just mechanical
  bonuses. A sword that glows near a specific bloodline is more
  interesting than a +1 sword.
- Cursed or double-edged items create dramatic tension. The
  best items have a cost or complication attached to their use.
- Artifacts and tomes should connect to existing campaign lore
  and factions.

### System-Specific Item Generation

**CoC 7e:** Items are often documents, tomes, or relics. Tomes
grant Cthulhu Mythos skill points at a SAN cost. Generate tomes
with: title, author, language, Mythos gain, SAN cost, study
time, spells contained, and physical description.

**D&D 5e (2024):** Magic items follow rarity tiers (Common,
Uncommon, Rare, Very Rare, Legendary). Generate with: rarity,
attunement requirement, properties, and flavour text. Weapon
mastery properties apply to martial weapons.

**GURPS 4e:** Items are defined by cost, weight, and mechanical
effects. Enchanted items use the GURPS Magic system with energy
cost and prerequisites.

**FitD:** Items are abstracted by quality (Tier 0-5). Generate
items with: Tier, type, special effect, and load value.

## Faction Generation

### Faction Template

```
## [Faction Name]

**Type:** [Cult / Guild / Government / Criminal / Military /
  Academic / Religious / Other]

**Concept:** [One-sentence summary of what they are]

**Goals:**
- Immediate: [What they are doing now]
- Long-term: [What they are working toward]

**Resources:** [What power and assets they command]

**Leadership:**
- [Leader NPC]: [Brief description and role]
- [Lieutenant(s)]: [Brief description and role]

**Membership:** [Size, recruitment, loyalty]

**Territory:** [Where they operate]

**Methods:** [How they pursue their goals]

**Relationships:**
- [Faction/Entity]: [Allied / Rival / Neutral / Hostile]

**Clock:** [What they are working toward, with segments]

**Secrets:** [What outsiders do not know]

**Hooks:** [How PCs encounter or interact with this faction]

**System Notes:** [Tier, status, or other system-specific data]
```

### Faction Design Principles

- Factions need goals that advance on a timeline whether PCs
  intervene or not. A static faction is wallpaper.
- Every faction should have at least one relationship (ally,
  rival, or neutral) with another existing faction.
- Factions should present moral complexity. Purely evil
  organisations are less interesting than organisations with
  understandable goals pursued through objectionable means.
- Include at least one internal tension (leadership dispute,
  resource shortage, ideological split) that PCs can exploit
  or that drives subplot.

## Document and Handout Generation

### In-Game Document Template

```
## [Document Title]

**Type:** [Letter / Diary / Newspaper / Map / Official Record /
  Tome / Note]

**Author:** [Who wrote it]

**Date:** [When it was written, in campaign time]

**Physical Description:** [Condition, materials, appearance]

**Content:**
[The actual text of the document, written in-character.
  For letters: salutation, body, closing.
  For diaries: dated entries.
  For newspapers: headline, byline, article body.
  For official records: formal language and structure.]

**What It Reveals:** [GM-only: what clues this contains]

**Discovery Method:** [How PCs find this document]

**System Notes:** [Any skill checks to read, translate, or
  interpret. Language requirements. SAN costs for Mythos texts.]
```

### Handout Design Principles

- Handouts should look and feel like the in-game object. A
  letter should read like a letter, not a game document.
- Vary handout types across a scenario (letters, newspapers,
  official records, personal journals) to maintain player
  interest.
- Limit handouts to 3-5 per session. More than that overwhelms
  players.
- Public documents (newspapers, posted notices) are hooks that
  can be provided freely. Personal documents (diaries, letters)
  should be gated behind investigation or interaction.
- Every handout must contain at least one clue or story-relevant
  piece of information. Flavour-only handouts waste table time.

## Scenario Outline Generation

### Scenario Template

```
## [Scenario Title]

**System:** [CoC 7e / D&D 5e / GURPS 4e / FitD]

**Premise:** [2-3 sentences: what is happening and why it
  matters]

**Hook(s):** [At least 3 hooks drawing PCs into the scenario]

**The Truth:** [GM-only: what is actually going on, stated
  plainly up front so the GM knows everything]

**Key NPCs:**
- [NPC 1]: [Role, goal, and connection to the truth]
- [NPC 2]: [Role, goal, and connection to the truth]

**Key Locations:**
- [Location 1]: [What happens here, what can be found]
- [Location 2]: [What happens here, what can be found]

**Node Map:**
[Description of how locations, NPCs, and events connect.
  Each node lists its clues pointing to other nodes.]

**Timeline:** [What happens if PCs do not intervene, with
  dates or triggers]

**Climax:** [The confrontation or revelation the scenario
  builds toward]

**Resolution:** [Possible endings and their consequences]

**Sequel Hooks:** [What future adventures this scenario
  enables]
```

### Scenario Design Principles

- State the truth in the first section. The GM must know
  everything from the start.
- Provide at least 3 hooks because PCs may ignore the first
  two.
- Use node-based design: self-contained nodes with clues
  pointing to other nodes, multiple paths through the scenario.
- Include a timeline of what happens without PC intervention.
  The world does not pause.
- Plan for failure states. What happens if the PCs lose?

## Tone Calibration

When generating content, match the register to the game system
and campaign tone:

**Cosmic Horror (CoC 7e):**
- Understated descriptions that imply more than they state.
- Mundane details that become unsettling in context.
- Academic or period-appropriate language for handouts.
- Escalating wrongness: things are slightly off, then very off.

**Heroic Fantasy (D&D 5e):**
- Bold, evocative descriptions with a sense of wonder.
- Clear stakes and rewards.
- Colourful NPC personalities.
- A sense that the world is large and full of possibility.

**Gritty Realism (GURPS 4e):**
- Precise, grounded descriptions with specific sensory detail.
- Consequences feel real and lasting.
- NPCs have mundane concerns alongside extraordinary ones.
- Technical accuracy in period and setting details.

**Criminal Underworld (FitD):**
- Noir-inflected descriptions: rain, shadows, gaslight.
- Every character has an angle.
- Locations feel lived-in and layered.
- Tension between ambition and survival.
